Racism

General/Misc.

The Color of Supremacy: Beyond the discourse of ‘white privilege’

  • Includes a massive list of ways in which black people are disadvantaged historically and today.

Encyclopedia of Political Economy: Racism

  • Unlike sex, race is a social category with little biological significance, often categorized on the basis of physical appearance with some exceptions like Jews. Although such classifications vary, they consistently establish patterns of domination and subordination.
  • The forms of racial oppression vary among societies, although are often characterized by the overt and dominant role of the state. The trend in the modern period is toward an open system where racial oppression becomes the ordinary workings of a capitalist economy.
  • Racism has existed long before capitalism, and capitalism has upheld it as well as provided the impetus for its destruction in some sense, with the ethos of individualism.
  • Gunnar Myrdal believed that the racism in capitalism reproduces itself in a cycle, where the exploitation of blacks lowered their standard of living and so reinforced the prejudice of whites which rekindled exploitation. However, he believed American individualism would eventually diminish the hold of racism, and also thought that appropriate government policies could improve QOL for blacks and thus reduce prejudice against them.
  • Marxists believe racism is a fundamentally economic problem and not just a psychological problem. However, the initial efforts of Marxists to argue that blacks were a super exploited sector of the working class proved unsuccessful as the relationship between wages and productivity could not be shown to be different for black and white workers, nor did efforts to describe the ghetto as an internal colony bear fruit.
  • Employers must avoid offending the sensibilities of their workers, suppliers, or customers to function effectively in the social environment, and therefore tend to establish a division of labor that mimics existing social hierarchies. The same is true with respect to the structure of the labor market as a whole, such as with the theory of segmented and dual labor markets.
  • Employers and white employees may also face incentives to behave in racist ways. Employers may be able to reduce black bargaining power through racism, in which case, whites would also be hurt through having their bargaining power reduced as well. However, the empirical evidence for this is weak, and whites may also benefit through less competition.
  • Other political economists have analyzed racism as a force which divides the labor market into racial/ethnic enclaves, as groups attempt to insulate themselves from job competition. The distributional effects vary with conditions. Nonetheless, a connection is often drawn between beneficiaries and blame for discrimination which can lay the basis for political judgement, particularly for white workers in the battle against racism.
  • Political economists tend to treat racism as a dynamic phenomenon that quantitatively and qualitatively varies depending on context. They also lay the seeds for optimism, suggesting that policies for full employment, as well as progressive changes in social attitudes, law, and public policy, can reduce racism.
  • The political economy analysis of racism has several limitations. First, it centers on the role of black workers rather than unemployed black people. However, political economists attribute problems of the underclass to lack of opportunities. Second, it tends to ignore forces such as trends in marital and birth patterns, child rearing arrangements, labor market restructuring and inner-city turmoil which have had more impact on black well-being than racism per se. Third, the patterns of inequality have become more complex, and blacks are no longer uniformly affected by racism. Finally, the analysis only looks at blacks rather than other groups.

Bias

Stereotypes/Self-Image

Does appearance matter?: the effect of skin tones on trustworthy and innocent appearances

  • Finds that undergraduate, entry-level criminal justice students judged perceived trustworthiness of darker-skinned people as less than of lighter-skinned people.

For Black men, being tall increases threat stereotyping and police stops

  • Finds that for black men, height increased the likelihood of receiving unjustified attention from the police, although white men may benefit from height

Black Neighbors, Higher Crime? The Role of Racial Stereotypes in Evaluations of Neighborhood Crime

  • Finds that the perception of crime in a neighborhood is positively associated with the percentage of young black people living there, even after controlling for crime and other neighborhood characteristics

Whites See Racism as a Zero-Sum Game That They Are Now Losing

  • White people are increasingly seeing racism as a zero-sum game, such that white people view decreases in perceived bias against blacks over the past 6 decades are associated with increases in perceived bias against whites, a relationship not observed in blacks’ perceptions.
  • These changes in whites’ conceptions of racism are extreme enough that whites have now come to view anti-white bias as a bigger societal problem than anti-black bias

Investigating the cognitive structure of stereotypes: Generic beliefs about groups predict social judgments better than statistical beliefs

  • Looks at generic vs statistical stereotypes
  • Finds that generic stereotypes are generally more influential on people’s expectations and explanations for unfamiliar individuals’ traits, and more so when the person displays an authoritarian personality and when they think intuitively rather than analytically

Effectiveness of Stereotype Threat Interventions: A Meta-Analytic Review

  • Meta-analysis of interventions to address stereotype threat
  • Finds that belief-based and identity-based stereotype threat interventions (changing the stereotypical beliefs about the group or group identification) are more effective in reducing stereotype threat

People See Black Men as Larger, More Threatening Than Same-Sized White Men

  • Finds that people see same-size black men as larger and more threatening than same-size white men.
  • Even black men were subject to this effect, although they didn’t see the black men as more threatening than the white men

Race and self-esteem: meta-analyses comparing whites, blacks, Hispanics, Asians, and American Indians and comment on Gray-Little and Hafdahl (2000)

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that blacks scored higher than whites in terms of self-esteem, but whites scored higher than Asians, HIspanics, and American Indians.
  • Most of these differences were small in childhood but grew with age
  • Blacks’ self-esteem was originally smaller than whites until the 1980s.
  • Relative to whites, minority males had lower self-esteem than minority females, and black and Hispanic self-esteem was higher in groups with high socioeconomic status
  • These results are most consistent with a cultural explanation for racial differences in self-esteem

The Structure of Ethnic Identity of Young Adolescents from Diverse Ethnocultural Groups

  • Finds that ethnic identity was associated positively with psychological well-being across racial groups

Improving intergroup attitudes and reducing stereotype threat: An integrated contact model

  • Argues that intergroup contact can be an effective method by which stereotype threat can be reduced.

The Essence of Innocence: Consequences of Dehumanizing Black Children

  • Finds that black children are seen as older and less innocent and prompt a less essential conception of childhood than white same-age peers.

Implicit Association Test

The implicit association test is not a good metric of any substantial level of racism, as the relationship between results on the test and explicit bias is small. Nonetheless, it has some predictive power.

A Systematic Review of the Impact of Physician Implicit Racial Bias on Clinical Decision Making

  • Systematic literature review
  • Finds that the highest-quality studies indicate that while physicians do exhibit an implicit racial bias for whites on the IAT, this does not appear to impact their clinical decision making.

A Meta-Analysis on the Correlation Between the Implicit Association Test and Explicit Self-Report Measures

  • Meta-analysis
  • Suggests that the relationship between implicit bias on the IAT and explicit bias is generally small

Predicting ethnic and racial discrimination: A meta-analysis of IAT criterion studies

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that the IAT is a poor predictor of every explicit measure examined except for brain activity.

Subconscious Bias

This section goes over a lot of somewhat random studies, but the main finding is that subconscious bias appears to exist against black people.

The development of intergroup bias in childhood: How social norms can shape children's racial behaviours

  • Finds that younger and older children will discriminate against black people, but older children will only do so under low salience of anti-racist norms, while younger children will do it in general.

Majority Of White Americans Say They Believe Whites Face Discrimination

  • Finds that 55% of whites believe anti-white discrimination happens in America, while 84% of whites think anti-minority discrimination happens in America.

Keep America Christian (and White): Christian Nationalism, Fear of Ethnoracial Outsiders, and Intention to Vote for Donald Trump in the 2020 Presidential Election

  • Finds that the strongest predictors of supporting Trump in order of magnitude were political party, xenophobia, identifying as African American (negative), political ideology, Christian nationalism, and Islamophobia

Prejudice Is Hard-wired Into The Human Brain, Says ASU Study

  • Finds that prejudice in the form of perceived physical and health danger is hard-wired into the human brain, through it being an adaptive response to perceived threat from outside groups.

Is Multiculturalism or Color Blindness Better for Minorities?

  • Finds that color-blind attitudes among dominant group members, compared to multicultural attitudes, reinforces majority group dominance and minority marginalization

Why Do Interracial Interactions Impair Executive Function? A Resource Depletion Account

  • Finds that interracial interactions impair executive functioning, likely due to resource depletion

https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20658843/

  • Finds that there exists social and economic penalties for counterstereotypic behavior, and this is dealt with by victims through defensive mechanisms which uphold stereotypes about their group identity.

Reactions to ethnic deviance: The role of backlash in racial stereotype maintenance

  • Finds that color-blind attitudes create a preference for individuals which permeates the boundaries of their group identity, while multiculturalism attitudes do not.

Attitudes Toward the Black Political Movement: Trends, Meaning, and Effects on Racial Policy Preferences

  • Finds that whites are less likely than black to support black political movements, and for many whites this is related to other conflict attitudes such as perceptions of conflicting group objectives and fraternal deprivation, all of which are strongly associated with prejudice.

https://www.jstor.org/stable/1519837?seq=1#metadata_info_tab_contents

  • Finds that the perception of black economic deprivation influences the prejudice of whites against blacks, and this exists even when whites understand the structural causes of such economic deprivation

Economic Disadvantage, Status Generalization, and Negative Racial Stereotyping by White Americans

  • Finds that stigmatized students who were high in stigmatized consciousness had lower GPA performance than stigmatized students who were low in stigmatized consciousness, who did not differ from non-stigmatized students

Racial Microaggressions and the Asian American Experience

  • Finds that Asians typically experience different types of microaggressions than other marginalized groups

Racial Microaggressions in Everyday Life: Implications for Clinical Practice

  • Microaggressions appear in the form of microassaults, microinsults, and microinvalidations
  • Almost all interracial encounrters are prone to microaggressions

Criminal Justice

Police Subconscious Bias

The police appear to have a subconscious bias against black people in terms of attributions about the causes of crime and general respect. These don’t appear to arise from overt bias but unexamined beliefs, which can be alleviated through better training and better collection and leveraging of data.

Language from police body camera footage shows racial disparities in officer respect

  • Uses footage from body-worn cameras and computational linguistic algorithms
  • Finds that officers speak with consistently less respect toward black vs white community members, even after controlling for the race of the officer, the severity of the infraction, the location of the stop, and the outcome of the stop.

Strategies for Change: Research Initiatives and Recommendations to Improve Police-Community Relations in Oakland, Calif.

  • Finds that OPD officers treat people of different races differently although these mostly arose from unexamined cultural norms, beliefs, and practices, as opposed to overt bias
  • Suggests that regular review of policies, practices, and procedures, better training of new officers, and better collection and leveraging data, can go a long way to reducing these disparities

Racial Disparities in Official Assessments of Juvenile Offenders: Attributional Stereotypes as Mediating Mechanisms

  • Finds that officers have differences in their attributions about the causes of crime by white vs minority youths, which contributes significantly to the differential sentencing outcomes that exist through sentencing recommendations

Use of Force

This section doesn’t distinguish between the validity of certain studies, and studies looking at use of force vary heavily in terms of their results. However, there is nonetheless a slant towards the idea that minorities do unjustifiably experience more use of force incidents, at the very least because the likelihood of encounters with the police is more common.

A Social Scientific Approach toward Understanding Racial Disparities in Police Shooting: Data from the Department of Justice (1980-2000)

  • Study of 213 metropolitan areas over a 21-year period
  • Finds that even after controlling for racial differences in criminal activity (a proxy for behavior), officers are still more likely to shoot black suspects

Risk of being killed by police use of force in the United States by age, race–ethnicity, and sex

  • Among black men, police use of force is one of the leading causes of death

Police-Related Deaths and Neighborhood Economic and Racial/Ethnic Polarization, United States, 2015–2016

  • Finds that police-related death rates are highest among neighborhoods which are lower in income and which have a higher proportion of residents of color there.

Police killings and their spillover effects on the mental health of black Americans: a population-based, quasi-experimental study

  • Finds that increased exposure to more police killings of unarmed black Americans recently decreases the mental health of Black americans.

Do White Law Enforcement Officers Target Minority Suspects?

  • Finds that although minority suspects are disproportionately killed by police, white officers appear to be no more likely to use lethal force against minorities than non-white officers.

An Empirical Analysis of Racial Differences in Police Use of Force

  • Finds that blacks and hispanics are disproportionately likely to experience both lethal and non-lethal use of force
  • Controlling for variables which take into account context and civilian behavior does not fully explain the disparity in non-lethal use of force, but does fully explain the lethal use of force disparity.

Race and Reasonableness in Police Killings

  • Finds that differences in circumstances do not explain the disparity between black and whites in police killings.
  • The standards in constitutional case law fail to anticipate the circumstances surrounding these types of interactions
  • Enhanced police training appears to be ineffective, likely because it does not take into account the importance of race in officers’ perceptions of risk and their decision-making.

A Multi-Level Bayesian Analysis of Racial Bias in Police Shootings at the County-Level in the United States, 2011–2014

  • Finds that racial bias is more likely to emerge in metropolitan areas with more black people and lower incomes, especially when there is high financial inequality in the county.
  • There was no relationship between bias and crime rates, suggesting that differential crime rates do not explain this bias.

Officer characteristics and racial disparities in fatal officer-involved shootings

  • Finds no racial disparity in shootings at all by officers
  • Officer race does not appear to affect this disparity
  • However, a retraction to the study states that this study does not say anything about whether there is a racial disparity in shootings at all by officers, only whether officer race has any effect.

Is There Evidence of Racial Disparity in Police Use of Deadly Force? Analyses of Officer-Involved Fatal Shootings in 2015–2016

  • Finds that looking at the racial disparity in shootings as a proportion of crime rather than population suggests that there is no racial disparity in fatal shootings by police.

Racial Disparities in Police Use of Deadly Force Against Unarmed Individuals Persist After Appropriately Benchmarking Shooting Data on Violent Crime Rates

  • Responds to the previous study suggesting that benchmarking police killing on relative crime rates rather than relative populations makes police use of force unbiased
  • Finds that controlling for this still creates a large and statistically significant disparity negatively affecting black people.

Does Race Matter for Police Use of Force? Evidence from 911 Calls

  • White officers increase use of force more than black officers when in minority neighborhoods, but not in white or mixed neighborhoods
  • Black and hispanic civilians are five times more likely to experience gun use of force by white officers compared to if white officers scaled up use of force similarly to black officers.

Police Use of Force as an Extension of Arrests: Examining Disparities across Civilian and Officer Race

  • Finds that black civilians are more likely to be involved in a use of force incident during an arrest, but this stems from differences in initial likelihood of arrest.

On Racial Disparities in Recent Fatal Police Shootings

  • Finds that a racial disparity in shooting victims persists even after controlling for local population and arrest demographics.
  • There is no evidence that police-worn body cameras impact the racial distribution of victims.

The Police Officer’s Dilemma: A Decade of Research on Racial Bias in the Decision to Shoot

  • Literature review
  • Finds that while police officers are affected by target race in some respects, they do not generally show a pattern of biased shooting, and this performance depends on the exercise of cognitive control, which allows officers to overcome the influence of stereotypes.

Resolution of apparent paradoxes in the race-specific frequency of use-of-force by police

  • Finds that while there is a racial bias against white individuals with officers' probability to shoot and use lethal force, this is reversed when accounting for the racial disparity in probability of police encounter.

The Reverse Racism Effect: Are Cops More Hesitant to Shoot Black Than White Suspects?

  • Experiment
  • Finds that while there is clear evidence of an implicit bias against black suspects by police, officers were slower to shoot armed black suspects than white suspects and less likely to shoot unarmed black suspects than unarmed white suspects.

Explaining the Disparity in Results Across Studies Assessing Racial Disparity in Police Use of Force: A Research Note

  • Literature review
  • Finds that there is high variation in findings of police use of force studies, even in laboratory settings. The variations could reflect variation in the actual phenomenon across agencies or geographic areas, or could reflect different research methods used to study the same phenomenon.

BLM

BLM protests have been overwhelmingly peaceful, may have had mixed effects on crime, and did not increase COVID in areas where they were.

A Year of Racial Justice Protests: Key Trends in Demonstrations Supporting the BLM Movement

  • The BLM movement has been overwhelmingly non-violent, with 94% of all BLM demonstrations being peaceful

Black Lives Matter's Effect on Police Lethal Use-of-Force

  • Finds that BLM protests have decreased police homicides and property crimes within the areas they are located, but have increased murders in general.
  • However, demographic imbalance in protest variation and treatment timing warrant a cautious interpretation

Black Lives Matter Protests and Risk Avoidance: The Case of Civil Unrest During a Pandemic

  • Finds that BLM protests did not cause rise in COVID within the areas where they were because they resulted in activities being averted which were more risky.

Death Penalty Racism

Race plays a significant role in the probability for a person to get the death penalty, all else equal.

The Role of Race in Washington State Capital Sentencing, 1981-2014

  • Looks at the role of race in washington state capital sentencing
  • Finds that although the race of defendant or victim does not affect prosecutorial decision-making, the death sentence is 4 times more likely to be imposed if the defendant is black

Race and Wrongful Convictions in the United States

  • Finds that despite making up only 13% of the American population, African Americans constitute 47% of the 1900 exonerations listed in the National Registry of Exonerations.

Looking Deathworthy: Perceived Stereotypicality of Black Defendants Predicts Capital-Sentencing Outcomes

  • Finds that after controlling for a wide array of factors, in cases involving a white victim, the more stereotypically a black defendant is perceived to be, the more likely they are to be sentenced to death.

Sentencing

There exists substantial sentencing biases against black people.

Criminalizing Race: Racial Disparities in Plea Bargaining

  • Finds that white defendants are 25% more likely than black defendants to have their initial charge dropped or reduced to a lesser crime.
  • However, white defendants and black defendants with prior convictions are treated the same
  • This suggests that prosecutors may be using race as a proxy for latent criminality and likelihood of recidivism

A Meta-Analysis of Race and Sentencing Research: Explaining the Inconsistencies

  • Meta-analysis of 71 studies
  • Finds that the magnitude of the racial disparity in sentencing between blacks and whites is statistically significant but small and highly variable

Arrests and Traffic Stops

Most studies suggest that there is unjust discrimination against black people in terms of arrests and traffic stops. While this does exist, there is a tradeoff nonetheless between racial equity in traffic stops and prevention of crime, suggesting some profiling can be justifiable, although much of it that happens now appears not to be. Black officers appear to be harsher against black suspects, decreasing black victimization but increasing the arrest rate of black suspects.

The Effects of Suspect Characteristics on Arrest: A Meta-Analysis

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that blacks, males, and Hispanics are more likely to be arrested than whites, females, and non-Hispanics.
  • These persisted among the majority of moderator categories

Understanding Racial and Ethnic Disparities in Arrest: The Role of Individual, Home, School, and Community Characteristics

  • Finds that black youth and young adults are significantly more likely to be arrested than whites in the same category, even after controlling for a range of delinquent behaviors.
  • This disparity disappears once the racial composition of the neighborhood is accounted for

Allocating Police Resources While Limiting Racial Inequality

  • There exists a tradeoff between racial equity in traffic stops and prevention of crime.
  • Therefore, one cannot get a proportional racial distribution of stops without large decreases in efficiency of targeting high crime areas

Reducing Racial Disparities in Crime Victimization

  • Despite what some argue is over-policing, black Americans are more likely to be victimized than white Americans.
  • Finds that post-litigation affirmative action in police departments increased the share of black officers
  • This reduced black victimization
  • This is likely due to increased responsiveness by police to black victimization

A large-scale analysis of racial disparities in police stops across the United States

  • Analyzes nearly 100 million traffic stops conducted across the US
  • Finds that black drivers are less likely to be stopped after sunset, when a veil of darkness clouds one’s face, suggesting bias in stop decisions
  • The bar for searching black and Hispanic drivers was lower than that for searching white drivers, although legalization of recreational marijuana decreases it somewhat

Race and Officer Decision Making: Examining Differences in Arrest Outcomes between Black and White Officers

Drug War

There is a racial disparity in drug arrests, but an important confound exists which makes it difficult to say whether this is racist in nature, namely the disparity in location of purchase. Weak evidence suggests this doesn’t account for the entire disparity, but more evidence in this section would be needed for a more confident conclusion.

Examining Racial Disparities in Drug Arrests

  • Finds that racial disparities exist in drug arrests, and that these cannot be explained by differences in drug offending, non-drug offending, or residing in the kinds of neighborhoods likely to have heavy police emphasis on drug offending.
  • This suggests that there exists racial bias in drug sanctions

Racial differences in marijuana-users' risk of arrest in the United States

  • Finds that while there exists a massive racial disparity in arrests for marijuana possession offenses, there also exists large disparities in buying outdoors, buying from a stranger, and buying away from homes.
  • The paper does do some back-of-the-envelope calculations which suggest that even controlling for this a disparity in arrests persists, but this is not conclusive whatsoever

Race/Ethnicity Differences in the Validity of Self-Reported Drug Use: Results from a Household Survey

  • Finds that African Americans are less trustworthy in their self-reported drug use compared to whites, even after controlling for potential mediators.

Race, Drugs, and Policing: Understanding Disparities in Drug Delivery Arrests

  • Finds that blacks are significantly overrepresented among drug arrestees in Seattle
  • This can be explained by law enforcement’s focus on crack offenders, the priority places on outdoor drug venues, and the geographic concentration of police resources in racially heterogeneous areas.
  • These practices are not determined by race-neutral factors such as crime rates or community complaints.

The War on Marijuana in Black and White

  • Finds that on average a black person is 3.73 times more likely to be arrested for possession of marijuana than a white person.
  • Moreover, this policing has not reduced marijuana usage or availability, and has been very fiscally costly.

Juries and Jury Selection

Juries appear to be highly racially biased against black people, although racial diversity in a jury removes this completely.

Different Shades of Bias: Skin Tone, Implicit Racial Bias, and Judgments of Ambiguous Evidence

  • Finds that showing participants a photo of a dark-skinned perpetrator introduced racial bias into evaluating evidence in a jury.
  • This predicted guilty/not guilty verdicts for the jury
  • These cues were likely implicit and unaware to the participants.

How Much Do We Really Know about Race and Juries? A Review of Social Science Theory and Research 

  • Literature review
  • Concludes that white participants are in fact more likely to be biased against black suspects
  • However, this often only shows up in cases where the situation is not racially charged to begin with
  • Black participants are more likely to be biased against black suspects in general

The Impact of Jury Race in Criminal Trials

  • Finds that all-white jury pools convict black defendants significantly more than white defendants, but this gap is entirely eliminated when the jury pool has at least one black member

Economics

Employment Discrimination

There is extensive empirical evidence suggesting there is employment discrimination against minorities. Specifications in names common among minorities cause variations in the level of discrimination. Ban-the-box policies and employers who advertise diversity hiring can perpetuate this discrimination, despite being well-intentioned. The reasons for this are partially due to statistical discrimination, and also partially due to taste discrimination. While the evidence for employment discrimination is very substantive, there is publication bias in the literature nonetheless.

Quantifying the Effect and Examining its Causes

(Quillian et al., 2017) Meta-analysis of field experiments shows no change in racial discrimination in hiring over time (Abstract and Appendix)

  • Meta-analysis of 28 studies
  • Finds that since 1989, whites on average receive 36% more callbacks from job applications than African Americans, and 24% more callbacks than Latinos
  • There was no change in the 25 years for African Americans, but there was for Latinos.
  • An appendix looked at how covariates, such as criminal record could affect the results, and concluded that “there are no trends in the covariates that appear potentially problematic for our results.”

(Zschirnt & Ruedin, 2016) Ethnic discrimination in hiring decisions: a meta-analysis of correspondence tests 1990–2015 (Full Paper)

  • Meta-analysis of 43 studies
  • Finds substantive evidence of racial discrimination in hiring against minority applicants of all races, and is also robust to the generation of an immigrant.
  • Given the similar hiring decisions for immigrants of different generations, this suggests that there is taste-based discrimination
  • However, statistical discrimination also appears to play a role, as shown by differences in German-speaking countries where more extensive application material is the norm and from public sector employers where application material is more standardized, there is more attention to diversity, and non-discriminatory hiring practices are often explicitly sought.

  • There is publication bias in the literature, as shown by the graph below (black dots are individual studies and white dots are results needed to balance the picture

Effect of Differing Names

(Gaddis, 2017) How Black Are Lakisha and Jamal? Racial Perceptions from Names Used in Correspondence Audit Studies (Abstract)

  • Survey experiment
  • Finds that names given more commonly by highly educated black mothers, such as Jalen and Nia, are less likely to be perceived as black than names given by less educated black mothers, such as DaShawn and Tanisha.
  • This suggests that evidence on racial discrimination operates under a false assumption that all black names are alike.

(Darolia et al., 2015) Race and gender effects on employer interest in job applicants: new evidence from a resume field experiment (Full Paper)

  • Resume experiment
  • Assigns names to the resumes that convey race and gender but for which a strong negative socioeconomic connotation is not implicated:
  • “We selected last names suggestive of race for black, Hispanic and white applicants. Applicants’ first names were used to convey gender, and in the case of Hispanic applicants also were indicative of ethnicity. Specifically, we used three female-sounding first names – Isabella, Megan and Chloe – and three male-sounding first names – Brian, Carlos and Ryan. The first names Isabella and Carlos were paired with Hernandez and Garcia to suggest Hispanic origin, while Chloe and Ryan were paired with Washington and Jefferson to indicate an African American applicant. For white applicants, the first names Megan and Brian were paired with Anderson and Thompson.”
  • Finds little evidence of systematic employer preferences for applicants from particular race or gender groups.

Effect of Diversity Hiring Policies

(Kang et al., 2016) Whitened Résumés: Race and Self-Presentation in the Labor Market (Abstract)

  • Many racial minorities will engage in the practice of whitening their resumes by concealing or downplaying racial cues in their job applications.
  • However, when targeting an employer that presents itself as valuing diversity, minority job applicants engage in relatively little resume whitening, but organizational diversity statements are not actually associated with reduced discrimination against unwhitened resumes
  • Therefore, minorities may be particularly likely to experience disadvantage when applying to ostensibly pro-diversity employers.

Effect of “Ban-the-Box” Policies

(Agan & Starr, 2016) Ban the Box, Criminal Records, and Statistical Discrimination: A Field Experiment (Abstract)

  • Looks at the effect that “Ban-the-Box” policies (policies which restrict employers from asking about applicants’ criminal histories on job applications) have on employment discrimination.
  • Finds that following the implementation of BTB policies in New Jersey and New York City the race gap goes from whites receiving 7% more callbacks than similar black applicants to a 45% difference.

Housing

There is a large amount of evidence that there is discrimination in housing loans and that black neighborhoods are devalued all else equal. Segregation during redlining has had lasting effects, with housing patterns being primarily driven by continuing prejudice and discrimination. This has resulted in black households struggling to make up lost ground relative to white households following the great recession.

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12552-019-09276-x

  • Looks at time series results from 16 field experiments of housing discrimination and 19 observational studies of mortgage lending discrimination
  • Finds evidence of a decline in housing discrimination from the late 1970s to the present
  • The decline is strongest for discrimination involving direct denial of housing availability, and weaker for measures reflecting the number of units recommended and inspected, and significant discrimination remains for these outcomes.
  • Racial gaps in loan denial have declined only slightly, and racial gaps in mortgage cost have not declined at all, suggesting persistent discrimination

https://www.aclu.org/files/field_document/discrimlend_final.pdf

  • Finds that among families that owned homes, white households have started to rebound from the worst effects of the great recession while black households are still struggling to make up lost ground.

https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0003122410380868

  • Finds that segregation was an important contributing cause to the foreclosure argument which was a highly racialized process that disproportionately negatively affected minority clients.

https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/2018.11_Brookings-Metro_Devaluation-Assets-Black-Neighborhoods_final.pdf

  • Finds that homes of similar quality in neighborhoods with similar amenities are worth 23% less in majority black neighborhoods, compared to those with very few or no black residents.
  • Metropolitan areas with greater devaluation of black neighborhoods are more segregated and have less upward mobility for the black children who grow up in those communities.

Is Perceived Racial Privilege Associated with Health? Findings from the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (Full Paper)

  • Used results from a survey of people in 7 states and Washington DC
  • Total sample of 22,412
  • Used regression analyses to find associations

Inequality as ingroup privilege or outgroup disadvantage: the impact of group focus on collective guilt and interracial attitudes (Full Paper)

Methods:

  • Study 1 surveyed 110 white students, each asked questions either along a white privilege framing or black disadvantage framing, and then people had questions asked along a scale meant to determine an individual’s level of racism. Regression analyses were done afterwards.
  • Study 2 surveyed 122 white students, and attempted to allow students to give more detail in their answers as opposed to on a specific point scale, to ensure the results in the first study weren’t due to differences between the two groups. Questions were still manipulated either along a white privilege framing or black disadvantage framing.

Findings:

  • Study 1 found that framing was a reliable predictor of collective guilt, with it being expressed more from those who had their given questions framed in terms of white privilege. Collective guilt explained the entire relationship between the framing and an individual’s level of racism, suggesting it is a mediator. Controlling for differential agreement ratings on questions did not significantly affect these relationships.

  • The results of study 2 did not differ significantly from those of study 1, although it was also found that those in the white privilege category expressed reliably less white racial identification.

Interpretations:

  • The relationship between framing, racism, and collective guilt is likely due to people who had the white privilege framing believing themselves to potentially play a more active role in the perpetuation of racism, and thus they held themselves more responsible for their actions.
  • The racial identification result was significant but weak, possibly due to participants perceiving little threat to the status of the ingroup.
  • It should be noted that collective guilt has been found in some circumstances to only predict a limited set of behavior, such as support for compensatory affirmative action policies but not for equal opportunity policies. This could be due to collective guilt being about past injustices which should be compensated as opposed to collective guilt about how the future may be.

Affirmative Action

Encyclopedia of Political Economy: Affirmative Action

  • Affirmative action is a term for policies that require taking positive actions to eliminate discrimination. It has been around since the 1940s but has become common in advanced capitalist countries since the 1970s.
  • Most of the opposition to affirmative action concerns how it entails making positive steps to create equality of opportunity for all, with many claiming it constitutes reverse discrimination. Opponents also claim it causes government interference in the labor market. 
  • A literature review found that affirmative action generally results in an improvement of the employment rate of black men. Another study found that there were more women and minorities in workforces subject to the requirements of affirmative action. However, this was progress towards proportionate representation, not reverse discrimination against white males.

Blackwell Encyclopedia of Sociology: Affirmative Action (Race and Ethnic Quotas)

  • Access to higher education, employment opportunities, and political quotas are the major arenas where affirmative action has been used. Differential group access to educational or employment positions is nothing new as powerful groups in most societies have tended to monopolize life chances, even in supposedly egalitarian societies, but affirmative action is prone to conflict because it represents an attempt to mitigate or reverse such inequalities.
  • The main advocates of affirmative action argue it is a necessary political initiative when group blind policies fail to produce significant results in rectifying past inequalities, as large inequality in accumulations of human and social capital, wealth, knowledge, and influence may take generations to overcome.
  • In democratic political systems where the majority is prone to bear the cost of such policies (such as the US as opposed to South Africa), the electoral backlash against them is a major obstacle. This is especially because there are immediate costs and long-term benefits that are not immediately seen.
  • Some critics of affirmative action argue that such policies are cosmetic and do not address fundamental problems generating inequality, potentially placing under qualified individuals in a position where they can fail to meet minimum performance standards or complete academic courses. This sets in motion a self-fulfilling prophecy and feeds the stereotypes of those opposed to greater assistance to the disadvantaged. 
  • Affirmative action policies also raise potentially difficult questions concerning inter-generational accountability, and individual as opposed to collective responsibility. While most accept the need to take some state action to compensate for past categorical discrimination, when specific individuals are confronted with situations where they lose out to what they see as “less qualified members” of other groups there is a tendency to interpret this as reverse racism.
  • Another issue is that about the social class impact, as it can be argued that some inclusion of formerly disadvantaged elites is an essential first step in reducing ethnic violence and conflict, also acting as vital role models to inspire hope and emulation. Opinions differ widely on whether affirmative action is the cause of or solution to ethnic and racial conflicts.
  • There may also be unintended benefits, such as pressures to expand opportunities, increasing the pool of talent, improvements in efficiency, and the encouragement of positive political mobilization. Critics however maintain that attempts to radically alter economic management and ownership inevitably produces incentives for bribery and corruption, with tokenism becoming common.
  • Another line of argument suggests public policy can be more effective in bringing about greater ethnic equality by using non-ethnic strategies, parallel to the advocates of class-based affirmative action. Such an approach avoids the question of group eligibility for preferential treatment, which there is much debate on.
  • A particularly vital factor is the economic context these policies are pursued in, as economic growth will decrease the disruption of affirmative action redistribution. Much of the success of the Malaysian case was due to economic growth. A major criticism of affirmative action is that they are introduced as temporary measures and then once started are politically impossible to end. India offers examples where this is supported, Malaysia suggests the reverse.

The Social Consequences of Affirmative Action: Deleterious Effects on Perceptions of Groups

Multiculturalism

Blackwell Encyclopedia of Sociology: Multiculturalism

  • The term multiculturalism emerged in the 1960s and 1970s with a policy focused often initially on schooling and the children of minority immigrants. In this context, multiculturalism meant the extension of the school in terms of curriculum and as an institution to teach about aspects of other cultures. In Canada and Australia, the focus was more broad from the start and also included constitutional and land issues in part due to questions of the entitlements and status of Inidgenous people in those countries.
  • The African American search for dignity has contributed much to this politics which has shifted attention from socioeconomic disadvantage, arguably where the need is greatest. Nathan Glazer has argued that African Americans as a group will not melt into American culture, not because of cultural difference, but because American society lacks the determination to combat the racism and severe disadvantage to make it happen. Thus, the rise of multiculturalism in the US is seen by him as a reflection of the lack of will to overcome the black-white divide. On this reading it is of no surprise that the US multiculturalist debate is primarily in higher education, where passion has been expended on arguments about curriculum, political correctness, and anxiety about the ethnicization of student dorms.
  • A fundamental division amongst scholars concerns the validity of cultural groups as a point of reference for multiculturalism, with the dominant view being that groups always have internal differences, and that to think otherwise would be to essentialize groups. Political theorists on the other hand continue to think of cultural groups as socio political actors who may bear rights and have needs that should be institutionally accommodated Empirical studies suggest both views have substance, as while many young people do not wish to be defined by a similar ethnicity there is also a development of distinct communities, usually ethnoreligious which sometimes seek corporate representation.
  • Multiculturalism has had a much less popular reception in Europe, where it has sometimes led to extreme nationalist parties winning control of some towns and cities. However, opposition to multiculturalism is not confined to extreme parties or even on the right. In France especially, some believe in a unified identity beyond race. The implosion of Yugoslavia with its ethnic cleansing marks the most extreme reaction to multiculturalism.
  • An additional charge against multiculturalism attacks explicit religious symbols such as bans on wearing “ostentatious” religious symbols like the hijab.

  • While Asians do well economically overall, there exists very large gaps between Asians based on origin country, in terms of income and poverty.

On the Similarities of American Blacks and Whites: A Reply to J. P. Rushton

  • Reviews the work of Phillippe Rushton, a prominent race realist psychologist
  • Finds that all of the claims he makes are based on erroneous evidence
  • Brain size differences between blacks and whites have been found to go in both directions when accounting for climatic zones
  • Brain size and intelligence are weakly correlated.
  • There is no consistent difference between black and whites in terms of brain/body ratio
  • Skin color would be a very weak predictor of crime due to high intra-group variance
  • His own data can be reinterpreted as relying on race as an indicator of crime leads to 99.8% false positives
  • The correlations between race and crime are too small and inconsistent to support genetic racial explanations
  • He erroneously relies on hospital admission rates to argue that black have more mental illness, which fails to take into account that these are biased against the poor as they are more likely to ignore confidentiality
  • More adequate studies find no difference except for simple phobias

Racial Differences in Addiction and Other Disorders Aren't Mostly Genetic

  • Finds that racial differences in addiction and other disorders are primarily due to socio economics rather than genetic differences between black people and white people

Are there racial and ethnic differences in psychopathic personality? A critique of Lynn's (2002) racial and ethnic differences in psychopathic personality

  • Large-scale community studies in America show no differences between races or ethnic groups in the diagnosis of antisocial personality disorder, nor in traits closely associated with psychopathy, sensation seeking and psychoticism.

Race and Intelligence

Racial IQ Differences among Transracial Adoptees: Fact or Artifact?

  • Argues that the alleged iq differences among transracial adoptees are not due to genetics but statistical error
  • The advantage of East Asians compared to whites is caused by ignoring the flynn effect and adoption’s beneficial effects on IQ, and most of the IQ disadvantage of black adoptees disappears when one allows for attrition in the Minnesota Transracial Adoption Study, and acknowledged the results of other studies
  • This suggests that there is a minimal role for genes in racial IQ differences
  • The small IQ gap between blacks and whites, the cause of which is disputed, does not change between the age of 7 and 17

Racial-group differences in IQ in the Minnesota Transracial Adoption Study: A reply to Levin and Lynn

  • Follow-up paper by the authors of the Minnesota Transracial Adoption Study
  • Argues that their study provides little to no conclusive evidence for genetic influences underlying racial differences in intelligence and achievement.

Racial-group differences in IQ in the Minnesota Transracial Adoption Study: A reply to Levin and Lynn

  • Argues that the overwhelming portion of literature on race, intelligence, and genetics, is based on folk taxonomies rather than actual scientific analysis
  • Thus, it serves social ends rather than scientific knowledge
  • Moreover, no gene has been conclusively linked to intelligence, so it is erroneous to attempt to find racial differences in intelligence at this time
  • Heritability is inadequate in regard to providing such a link.

No support for the hereditarian hypothesis of the Black–White achievement gap using polygenic scores and tests for divergent selection

  • Genome-wide association study
  • Finds that natural selection explains at best a substantially smaller iq gap between black and whites than hereditarians claim, even under unrealistic assumptions that overestimate genetic contribution, and that the evidence is consistent with genetic differences contributing little to the iq gap

Absence of a relationship between degree of white ancestry and intellectual skills within a black population

  • Finds no evidence of a statistically significant correlation between degree of African ancestry and intelligence.

Global Ancestry and Cognitive Ability

  • Finds a correlation between European ancestry and cognitive ability, independent of socioeconomic status and measures of eye, hair, and skin color.

Race, genetics and pseudoscience: an explainer

  • There is no definitive way to determine to what degree genetics or environment accounts for existing racial IQ gaps, because of gradients in genetics and culture and entanglements with genetics, ancestry, culture, and socioeconomics.
  • However, there are some pieces of evidence which suggest that they are primarily environmental
  • Firstly, the genes present within whites which are said to be most strongly associated with IQ are also present within blacks
  • Secondly, socioeconomics has played a strong role in IQ
  • Finally, the Flynn effect strongly suggests that much of IQ is environmentally determined

Stereotype Threat and the Intellectual Performance of African Americans

  • Finds that th5e existence of stereotypes about the intelligence of black people can impair their cognitive ability.

Predicting the conclusions of Negro—White intelligence research from biographical characteristics of the investigator

  • Study of data from 82 investigators
  • Finds that 7 biographical items of the author conducting the questionnaire predicted 45% of the variance in results of the iq gap between blacks and whites, and the proposed causes for it.

The relationship between IQ and climatic variables in African and Eurasian countries

  • Looks at iq in 128 countries in Africa, sia, and Europe
  • Finds that nasal index, lower precipitation, and lower temperature were all positively associated with higher iq

Survey of Expert Opinion on Intelligence: Causes of International Differences in Cognitive Ability Tests

  • Surveys expert opinions of the causes in differences in iq between countries
  • Finds that the top cause said was education, and the second highest was genetics, although there was very high variability in ratings
  • Culture, wealth, modernization, and politics were viewed as less important
  • There was little importance assigned to geography, climate, test bias, and sampling error

Equal opportunity and racial differences in IQ

  • Finds that equal opportunity to information that is going to be tested in an iq test eliminates the black-white iq gap

IQ test performance of Black children adopted by White families

  • Looks at the adoption of 130 black and interracial children from advantaged white families
  • The socially classified black adoptees, whose natural parents were educationally average, scored above the mean iq and school achievement of the white population
  • Biological children of the adoptive parents scored even higher
  • This suggests malleability of IQ under rearing conditions relevant to the tests and schools.

Black/White IQ Differences: Does Age Make the Difference?

  • While the standard deviation IQ difference between black and white adults has remained constant, IQ differences between black and white children are declining

Biogeographic Ancestry, Cognitive Ability and Socioeconomic Outcomes

  • Finds that there exists a relationship between genetic ancestry and both general cognitive ability and socioeconomic status
  • After controlling for socioeconomic status, the relationship between genetic ancestry and cognitive ability holds

The Meaningfulness of Negro-White Differences in Intelligence Test Performance

  • Finds that the correlation between skin color and intelligence is so small as to be meaningless in making any prescriptions for differential treatment of blacks and whites

Why national IQs do not support evolutionary theories of intelligence

  • Argues that correlating estimates of national iq with reproductive strategies, temperature, and geographic distance from Africa fails to support evolutionary theories of intelligence
  • These studies ignore the flynn effect, and their results are strongly confounded with the current developmental status of countries

Taboo of Race and IQ

The Mythical Taboo on Race and Intelligence

  • Argues that given the extensive literature on the subject of hereditarian research on race differences in intelligence, that the idea that taboo and suppression of such research is largely a myth.

Wealth of Nations

Does the intelligence of populations determine the wealth of nations?

  • Finds that differences in the timing of agriculture transition and the histories of states, not population iq differences, predict international development differences before the colonial era
  • The average iq of populations appears to be a product of the diverse stages of nations’ modernization, rather than causing economic development

Race and sex differences in head size and IQ

  • Finds that there are both race and sex differences in head size, although the sex difference in IQ is negligible, while white and black children who are matched on IQ show on average virtually no difference in head size

Meta-analysis of associations between human brain volume and intelligence differences: How strong are they and what do they mean?

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that head size correlates with IQ
  • However, brain size is not a necessary cause for human IQ differences, and it is not warranted to interpret brain size as a proxy of human intelligence differences

An empirical, 21st century evaluation of phrenology

  • Finds that scalp morphology cannot be used to infer levels of brain function in the healthy population

Gender and Sex

Gendered Behavior

Men and women appear to engage in different behaviors across time, culture, and age. This appears to suggest a possible biological explanation for it but the results also hint at stereotypes and different social expectations as a partial explanation for gender gaps in behavior.

Preschoolers’ Perceptions of Gender Appropriate Toys and their Parents’ Beliefs About Genderized Behaviors: Miscommunication, Mixed Messages, or Hidden Truths?

  • Studied 3- to 5-year-old children
  • Found that in spite of evidence that many of their parents reject common stereotypes, their children predicted parents would consistently apply these stereotypes in terms of approval or disapproval of children’s choice to play with gender-stereotyped or cross-gender toys.

The Biological Contributions to Gender Identity and Gender Diversity: Bringing Data to the Table

  • Literature review
  • Hypothesizes that gender identity is a multifactorial trait with a heritable polygenic component

Evaluating Gender Similarities and Differences Using Metasynthesis

  • Meta-synthesis
  • Finds that the average absolute difference between males and females across domains was relatively small, with the majority of effects being either small or very small.
  • The magnitude of differences fluctuated somewhat as a function of the psychological domain (cognitive variables, social and personality variables, well-being), but remained largely constant across age, culture, and generations.
  • The distributions of men and women on most variables overlap by about 84%, although the present findings cannot pinpoint the shape of these distributions.
  • Although gender differences are typically small when talking about measures of central tendency, they are more pronounced when comparing the tails of male and female distributions, as can be seen when comparing violence
  • Research also suggests that males are more variable than females on some intellectual and cognitive variables

Gender differences in competitiveness

  • Literature review that looks as gender differences in competitiveness
  • Suggests that multiple influences shape gender attitudes during different periods of the life cycle, and that these will not only influence outcomes during working age, but also during early childhood education.
  • This gender gap is rather fluid in nature and can be affected when using the right tool
  • The main sources of this difference are still under debate
  • Closing the gender gap might not be desirable in all circumstances, as men are often found to be overconfident and over-competitive.
  • Gender gaps in competition have been found to play a prominent role in subject choices for students

Men and Things, Women and People: A Meta-Analysis of Sex Differences in Interests

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that men prefer working with things and women prefer working with people.

Basic Biology

If defining intersex as any disorder of sex development, it is a fairly common occurrence. Due to this, any social expectations put on people on a binary level is reductive even presupposing that sex as a concept exists, given the bimodal nature of sexual characteristics.

Beyond XX and XY: The Extraordinary Complexity of Sex Determination

Sex & Gender

  • From the National Institutes of Health
  • Affirms the distinction between sex and gender, where sex is biological differences between females and males, while gender is socially constructed and enacted roles and behaviors which occur in a historical and cultural context and vary across societies and over time.

How sexually dimorphic are we? Review and synthesis

  • If looking at variances in sex chromosome composition, gonadal structure, hormone levels, and the structure of internal genital duct systems and external genitalia, the frequency of intersex people who deviate from the ideal male or female may be as high as 2% of live births.

Psychological Sex Differences

There are a wide variety of psychological sex differences in terms of aggression, personality, and mating patterns. At least some of these, mainly the mating patterns, are biological in origin, although much of it appears to also be due to gender roles.

Gender and aggressive behavior: A meta-analytic review of the social psychological literature

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that men are somewhat more aggressive than women on average, particularly for aggression that produced physical injury or pain compared with aggression that produced psychological or social harm
  • This is a function of perceived consequences of aggression that are learned as aspects of gender roles and other social roles.

Gender differences in personality: a meta-analysis

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that males are more assertive and have slightly higher self-esteem than females.
  • Females were higher than males in extraversion, anxiety, trust, and nurturance
  • There were no differences in social anxiety, impulsiveness, activity, reflectiveness, locus of control, and orderliness
  • These differences were generally constant across ages, time, educational levels, and nations

Gender Differences in Verbal Ability: A Meta-Analysis

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds a slight female advantage in verbal ability, although this is very small
  • There were no striking changes in the magnitude of this at different ages

Psychological sex differences: Origins through sexual selection

  • Argues that psychological sex differences exist because of differing evolutionary pressures put onto men and women
  • These can manifest as a result of paternity uncertainty, identification of reproductively valuable women, gaining sexual access to women, and identifying men who are able and willing to invest
  • This explains the lack of a difference of cognitive abilities except in the domain of spatial rotation, essential to successful hunting
  • This also explains differences in attitudes towards sex and preferences for mates

Transgenderism

In general, support for transgender people is positive for transgender people with no negative effects on society more broadly. Gender-affirming therapies improve the mental health of gender dysphoric people, and can even completely eradicate gender dysphoria, although care should be taken to ensure that it is the right treatment for any individual person. Bathroom bills, anti-discrimination legislation, and other similar legislation is positive for transgender people with no negative effects for society more broadly, although there may be a lack of fairness with allowing transgender women in women’s sports in at least some cases. Nonetheless, anti-transgender discrimination still persists strongly, which is a cause for the high suicidality seen in transgender populations today.

General/Misc.

This section just contains general information about transgender people that cannot be put into any other category. One thing of note in particular is how transgender children strongly identify with the role of their current gender as opposed to the role typically associated with their biological sex. Moreover, this section discusses the recommended clinical management of gender dysphoric people.

(Gulgoz et al., 2019) Similarity in Transgender and Cisgender Children's Gender Development

  • Study of 317 transgender children
  • Finds that transgender children strongly identify as members of their current gender group and show gender-typed preferences and behaviors strongly associated with their current gender
  • Transgender and cisgender children’s patterns of gender development were coherent
  • There were minimal or no differences in gender identity or preferences as a function of how long transgender children lived as their current gender.

(Olson et al., 2015) Gender Cognition in Transgender Children

  • Study of 32 prepubescent transgender children
  • Finds that early in development, transgender youth are statistically indistinguishable from cisgender children of the same gender identity

(Todd et al., 2019) Demographic and Behavioral Profiles of Nonbinary and Binary Transgender Youth

  • Finds that there are many similarities and disparities between nonbinary and binary transgender youth

(De Waal & Kettenis, 2006) Clinical Management of Gender Identity Disorder in Adolescents: A Protocol on Psychological and Pediatric Endocrinology Aspects

  • Goes through the clinical management of gender identity disorder in adolescents.
  • Treatment outcomes in transsexuals is expected to be more favorable when puberty is suppressed than when treatment is started after Tanner stage 4 or 5.
  • Suppression of puberty should be considered as supporting the diagnostic procedure of gender identity disorder, but not as the ultimate treatment, as it can be discontinued if the patient no longer pursues sex reassignment.
  • Otherwise, cross-sex hormone treatment can be 16 years, if there are no indications otherwise.
  • Puberty blockers consist of hormones to suppress development of irreversible sex characteristics of the unwanted sex.
  • Cross-sex steroid hormones are added to that medication from the age of 16.
  • A decrease in height velocity and bone maturation occurs
  • Body proportions remain in the normal range
  • Total bone density remains in the same range during the years of puberty suppression, whereas it significantly increases on cross-sex steroid hormone treatment

(UCLA, 2016) How Many Adults Identify as Transgender in the United States?

  • Finds that 0.5% of individuals 65 and older identify as transgender in the US, compared to 0.7% of individuals 18-24 years old.
  • This varies substantially by state

Blackwell Encyclopedia of Sociology: Transgender, Transvestism, and Transsexualism

  • American conceptualizations of transgender originate in the late 19th century, when psychiatrists and other medical practitioners began to puzzle over the nature of people who felt like/dressed as/behaved like a member of the opposite sex.
  • The term transvestite originated in 1910 to refer to men who enjoyed behaving and dressing as women or wished to be women. The term transsexual was coined in 1923, and the first full MTF “sex change” operation (vaginoplasty, following castration and penile amputation) happened in 1931. It was not until the early 1950s that the exact distinction between transvestites and transsexuals was adopted to distinguish between those seeking to change sex from those who were content to crossdress.
  • In the early 1970s the term gender dysphoria entered the literature and quickly became the preferred term used, but it was the term gender identity disorder which became enshrined in the official diagnostic nomenclature.
  • Janice Raymond’s work dominated discussions of the political significance of transsexualism during the 1980s, arguing that the creation of the male medical profession of transsexualism and its treatment by means of sex change surgery obscures the political and social sources of the transsexual’s suffering, treating it as an individual problem rather than a systemic problem. Raymond sees MTF transsexuals and in particular those who identify as lesbian feminists as part of a masculine attempt to undermine feminism.
  • By the late 1980s, some transvestites and transsexuals were beginning to use an umbrella term, “transgender”, and other gender variant people, such as drag queens and intersex people soon were lumped in.
  • One option not covered by the categories of transsexualism and transvestism was the possibility of living as a member of the other sex without undergoing genital surgery. This was the option for most FTM transsexuals because of the inability to create a satisfactory penis.
  • Drag kings entered the academic literature in the late 1980s, being viewed not simply as female counterparts to drag queens, but as a much more subversive phenomenon because of the mainstream view of masculinity as non-performative.
  • A postmodernist approach to gender was developed in the 1990s which held transsexuals as outside the boundaries of gender, suggesting there is a third space outside the gender dichotomy for transgender people, in which case the idea of permanent core identities disappears. Despite this, however, the binary view of gender prevailed at the time.

Transition

Medical Transition

Medical transition appears to improve transgender people’s quality of life, mental health, and significantly reduces or even eliminates gender dysphoria in people. Experiences of regret following medical transition are rare. Sexual health also appears to be improved, although there is evidence of suboptimal sexual functioning. It is important to note still, that the evidence base is fairly weak for a variety of reasons inherent to studying this topic.

Effects on Mental and General Health

(Murad et al., 2009) Hormonal therapy and sex reassignment: a systematic review and meta-analysis of quality of life and psychosocial outcomes (Abstract)

  • Meta-analysis of 28 studies on sex reassignment and hormonal therapy
  • All studies were observational and most lacked controls
  • Finds that most individuals with gender identity disorder reported significant improvement in gender dysphoria, psychological symptoms, quality of life, and sexual function.
  • However, this is acknowledged to be a very low quality evidence base

(Cohen-Kettenis & Goozen, 1997) Sex reassignment of adolescent transsexuals: a follow-up study (Abstract)

  • Study of 22 patients aged 16-18 1 year after undergoing sex reassignment.
  • Finds that postoperatively the group was no longer gender dysphoric, and were psychological and socially normal.
  • None experienced feelings of regret

(Johansson et al., 2010) A Five-Year Follow-Up Study of Swedish Adults with Gender Identity Disorder (Abstract)

  • Study of 42 transsexuals who completed a follow-up assessment 5 or more years after completed sex reassignment surgery
  • Clinicians rated the global outcome for their patients as favorable in 62% of the cases, compared to 95% of the patients themselves.

(Ruppin & Pfafflin, 2015) Long-Term Follow-Up of Adults with Gender Identity Disorder (Abstract)

  • Conducted follow-up interviews for 71 participants 10-24 years after the legal recognition of their gender change.
  • Patients reported high degrees of well-being and a good social integration
  • Moreover, they showed significantly fewer psychological problems and interpersonal difficulties as well as a strongly increased life satisfaction at follow-up than at the time of the initial consultation.

Sexual Health

Sexual Experiences in Transgender People: The Role of Desire for Gender-Confirming Interventions, Psychological Well-Being, and Body Satisfaction (Abstract)

  • Surveys 325 MtF and 251 FtM transgender adults
  • Finds that while gender-confirming treatment (GCT) may positively affect sexual feelings, particularly in MtF people, body satisfation may play an even bigger role. Those without a GCT desire may experience particular difficulties in their sexual experiences.

Sex reassignment of adolescent transsexuals: a follow-up study (Abstract)

  • Finds that transgender women post-treatment have better physical and mental health. 
  • However, sexual functioning was suboptimal, especially in terms of arousal, lubrication, and pain.

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10508-005-7926-5 (Abstract)

  • Long-term follow-up study of 55 transsexual patients
  • Finds that a large number of transsexuals (80%) reported improvement of their sexuality, although their expectations sexually were fulfilled to a lesser degree than on a social and emotional level.

Social Transition

Social transition improves the mental health of transgender people.

(Connolly et al., 2016) The Mental Health of Transgender Youth: Advances in Understanding (Abstract)

  • Systematic literature review of 15 studies
  • Finds that transgender youth have higher rates of depression, suicidality and self harm, and eating disorders compared to their peers.
  • Gender-affirming therapy and supported social transition in childhood correlate with improved psychosocial functioning for trans children and adolescents.

(Russell et al., 2018) Chosen Name Use Is Linked to Reduced Depressive Symptoms, Suicidal Ideation, and Suicidal Behavior Among Transgender Youth

  • Finds that after adjusting for personal characteristics and social support, chosen name in more contexts was associated with lower depression, suicidal ideation, and suicidal behavior.

(Olson et al., 2022) Gender Identity 5 Years After Social Transition

  • Looks at rate of retransition and gender identities after 5 years among 317 initially transgender youth with a mean starting age of 8.1.
  • Finds that 7.3% of youth retransitioned at least once, with later cisgender identities becoming more common among youth whose initial social transition was before 6 years of age. These retransitions often occurred before age 10.

Puberty Blockers/Hormone Therapy

Puberty blockers and hormone therapy have generally positive mental health effects for transgender people.

(Nobili et al., 2018) Quality of life of treatment-seeking transgender adults: A systematic review and meta-analysis (Abstract)

  • Systematic review and meta-analysis of 29 studies and 14 studies respectively
  • Transgender people report poorer mental health QoL compared to other populations
  • However, meta-analysis suggested that there is no difference between transgender people and other populations post-cross sex hormonal treatment.

(Reisner et al., 2016) A Systematic Review of the Effects of Hormone Therapy on Psychological Functioning and Quality of Life in Transgender Individuals (Abstract)

  • Systematic review of 3 studies
  • Finds that limited evidence supports the hypothesis that hormone therapy has positive effects on the mental health of transgender individuals.

(Fighera et al., 2019) Bone Mass Effects of Cross-Sex Hormone Therapy in Transgender People: Updated Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (Abstract)

  • Meta-analysis of 19 studies
  • Finds that long-term cross-sex hormone therapy (CSHT) has neutral effects on bone mineral density in trans men but had effects on only lumbar spine bone mineral density after CSHT.

(Weinand & Safer, 2015) Hormone therapy in transgender adults is safe with provider supervision; A review of hormone therapy sequelae for transgender individuals (Abstract)

  • Systematic literature review
  • Finds that hormone therapy is safe when followed carefully for certain risks, which do exist for both transgender men and women.
  • There appears to be no increase in cancer prevalence or mortality due to transgender hormone therapy

(Unger, 2016) Hormone therapy for transgender patients (Abstract)

  • Cross-sex hormone therapy has positive physical and psychological effects, although there are considerations to be made concerning bone and cardiovascular health
  • Care should be taken to monitor certain metabolic indices while patients are on cross-sex hormone therapy

(Karalexi et al., 2020) Gender-affirming hormone treatment and cognitive function in transgender young adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis (Abstract)

  • Meta-analysis of 10 studies
  • Finds that gender-affirming hormones have no adverse cognitive effects, but has positive visuospatial effects.

Puberty Blockers

(Meisen et al., 2020) Psychological Functioning in Transgender Adolescents Before and After Gender-Affirmative Care Compared With Cisgender General Population Peers (Abstract)

  • Study of hundreds of adolescents
  • Finds that transgender adolescents show poorer psychological well-being before puberty suppression but show equal or better well-being after compared with cisgender peers.

 (Rew et al., 2020) Review: Puberty blockers for transgender and gender diverse youth-a critical review of the literature (Abstract)

  • Systematic literature review of 9 articles
  • Finds positive outcomes of puberty blockers administered during tanner stages 2-4 included decreased suicidality in adulthood, improved affect and psychological functioning, and improved social life
  • Adverse outcomes include changes in body composition, slow growth, decreased height velocity, decreased cost of turnover, cost of drugs, and insurance coverage.
  • However, there was a lack of rigorous research in general, including no longitudinal research whatsoever included in the review

Detransition/Regret

Detransition and regret in transgender patients is extremely low in prevalence, although it does happen. Often, detransition is due to lack of social support as opposed to regretting the surgery per se.

Regret after Gender-affirmation Surgery: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Prevalence

  • Meta-analysis of 27 studies
  • Finds that there is an extremely low prevalence of regret in transgender payients after gender-affirmation surgeries (<1% for trans-masculine and 1% for trans-feminine).

Sex Reassignment. Thirty Years of International Follow-up Studies After Sex Reassignment Surgery: A Comprehensive Review, 1961-1991

  • Review of studies on the subject from 1961-1991
  • Finds that gender-reassigning treatments are effective, and desired results outweigh undesired effects.
  • The percentage of satisfactory results has only increased over time, and the percentage of unsatisfactory results has decreased.

Factors predictive of regret in sex reassignment

  • Looks at factors predictive of regret in sex reassignment
  • Finds that the 2 predictors were belonging to the non-core group of transsexuals and lack of support from the patient’s family.

Social Support

Perceived Discrimination, Social Support, and Quality of Life in Gender Dysphoria

  • Study of 94 trans men
  • Finds that social support from friends and family positively predicts quality of life, while perceived group discrimination had no effect on quality of life.

What does the scholarly research say about the link between family acceptance and LGBT youth well-being?

  • Literature review of 42 studies
  • Finds that family acceptance in general is linked to positive well-being of LGBT youth, and that accepting behavior of their orientation or identity is also linked to positive well-being.

Chosen Name Use Is Linked to Reduced Depressive Symptoms, Suicidal Ideation, and Suicidal Behavior Among Transgender Youth

  • Study of 129 transgender youths
  • Finds that after adjusting for personal characteristics and social support, chosen name use in more contexts was associated with lower depression, suicidal ideation, and suicidal behavior.

Mental Health

There are a large number of predictors of suicidality among transgender people, both general and transgender-specific. Discrimination and social rejection appear to increase suicidality, while social acceptance, anti-discrimination laws, and gender-affirming therapy where appropriate appear to decrease suicidality.

(McNeil et al., 2017) Suicide in trans populations: A systematic review of prevalence and correlates (Abstract)

  • Systematic review of 30 articles
  • Finds that discrimination increased suicidal ideation and attempts attempts of transgender people, while positive social interactions and timely access to interventions had positive effects.
  • However, results clearly indicated a need to improve on individual and structural levels.

(Herman et al., 2019) SUICIDE THOUGHTS AND ATTEMPTS AMONG TRANSGENDER ADULTS Findings from the 2015 U.S. Transgender Survey

  • Finds that general suicidality risk factors for transgender adults are psychological distress, alcohol or illicit drug use (excluding marijuana), and poor general health.
  • Unique suicidality risk factors include discrimination, familial rejection, conversion therapy, religious community rejection, experiences of violence, detransitioning, those who are not viewed as transgender (lower), those who do not disclose to others that they are transgender (lower), and minority stress.
  • Supportive families, hormone therapy and/or surgical care, and living in a state with gender identity nondiscrimination laws all reduced suicide.

Perception and Depiction

Negative media depictions of transgender people appear to result in a negative quality of life for transgender people. Moreover, online media appears to be better in terms of portraying transgender people than traditional media.

You Can Form a Part of Yourself Online: The Influence of New Media on Identity Development and Coming Out for LGBTQ Youth

  • Survey of 19 LGBTQ youth
  • Finds that online media enables LGBTQ youth to access resources, explore identity, find likeness, and digitally engage in coming out.

Perspectives of LGBTQ emerging adults on the depiction and impact of LGBTQ media representation

  • Survey of 19 LGBTQ adults
  • Finds that while traditional media, especially television creates a common dialogue and validates identity, it represents transgender people as homogenous, stereotypical, and limiting for young LGBTQ people’s perceptions of their future trajectories.
  • In contrast, new media offers new, important, and valued spaces for discussion and creativity.

Negative Transgender-Related Media Messages Are Associated with Adverse Mental Health Outcomes in a Multistate Study of Transgender Adults

  • Survey of 545 transgender adults
  • Finds that exposure to negative media depictions of transgender people is significantly associated with clinically significant symptoms of depression.

Politics

Bathroom Bills and Housing

Bathroom bills and gender-inclusive housing appear to have a positive effect on the quality of life of transgender people, both in terms of risk of sexual assault and suicidality. Moreover, there is no evidence that bathroom bills will increase the number or frequency of criminal incidents in bathrooms.

Transgender teens with restricted bathroom access at higher risk of sexual assault

  • Survey of nearly 3700 teens aged 13-17

    Comparing gendered and generic representations of mental illness in Canadian newspapers: an exploration of the chivalry hypothesis

    • Looks at how violence is reported in the British print media
    • Finds that women’s violence was more likely to be reported as irrational or emotional, with real wickedness ascribed in a few high-profile cases, while men’s violence was more likely to be presented as normal or rational.

    Workplace Gender Gaps

    This section covers gender gaps in the workplace in general. It appears that discrimination occurs against men in female-dominated workplaces, and women in male-dominated workplaces, likely due to stereotypes which exist. However, there also exist selection pressures which can be due to biology or sexism which result in men being more likely to even go into the higher-paying and more male-dominated careers. Interestingly, most workplace fatalities are from men.

    An Analysis of Reasons for the Disparity in Wages Between Men and Women

    • Finds that the adjusted pay gap is between 4.8-7.1%
    • This is after adjusting for human capital development, work experience, the occupations and industries in which they work, and interruptions in their careers
    • Other unadjusted factors include health insurance, other fringe benefits, and detailed features of overtime work.
    • It is possible that there will never be a reliable determination of whether any portion of the observed gender wage gap is not attributable to factors that compensate women and men differently on socially acceptable bases, and hence can be confidently attributed to overt discrimination against women.

    Fewer Women Than Men Die of Work-Related Injuries, Data Show

    • Despite making up 46% of the American’s employed workers, women accounted for just 8% of the nation’s job-related fatalities in 1992-94

    Science faculty’s subtle gender biases favor male students

    • Randomized double-blind study of 127 science faculty from research-intensive universities, who rated the application materials of a student, randomly assigned a male or female name, for a laboratory manager position
    • Finds that participants rated the male applicant as significantly more competent and hireable, also selecting a higher starting salary and offered more career mentoring to the male student

    What Do Field Experiments of Discrimination in Markets Tell Us? A Meta Analysis of Studies Conducted since 2000

    • Meta-analysis of field experiments on discrimination
    • Finds high levels of discrimination against men applying to female jobs

    Why Do Women’s Fields of Study Pay Less? A Test of Devaluation, Human Capital, and Gender Role Theory

    • Finds that the reason for fields with mostly men paying more than fields with mostly women is because there exist gendered patterns of self-selection to go into higher-paying fields.

    Encouraging women’s labor force participation in transition countries

    • Women’s participation across transition countries is driven by traditional views of gender roles and limited government support for caregivers
    • Increasing such participation can potentially offset the negative economic consequences of aging populations
    • Increased female entrepreneurship and the development of the care sector can help in increasing women’s labor force participation

    The underrepresentation of women in science: differential commitment or the queen bee syndrom?

    • Finds that among doctoral students there is no gender difference in work commitment or work satisfaction.
    • However, faculty members in the same university perceived female students to be less committed to their work and female faculty endorsed these gender-stereotypical perceptions most strongly

    When fit is fundamental: Performance evaluations and promotions of upper-level female and male managers

    • Finds that women are held to stricter standards for promotion, as their performance ratings when promoted were higher than for men.

    Evidence that gendered wording in job advertisements exists and sustains gender inequality

    • Finds that job advertisements for male-dominate areas included greater masculine wording (words associated with male stereotypes) than advertisements within female-dominated areas.
    • No difference in the presence of feminine wording emerged across these areas.
    • Participants perceive more men in jobs with advertisements that have more masculine than feminine wording, and women found these jobs less appealing.
    • Perceptions of belongingness but not perceived skills mediated the effect of gendered wording on job appeal.

    Prejudice against Women in Male-congenial Environments: Perceptions of Gender Role Congruity in Leadership

    • Looks at if there is a gender advantage in leadership
    • Finds that participants who evaluated candidates for a leadership position showed prejudice against the female candidate, especially when she worked in an industry incongruent with her gender role.
    • Female and older participants showed more prejudice against the female leader than did male and younger participants.

    The Glass Escalator: Hidden Advantages for Men in the "Female" Professions

    • Looks at men’s underrepresentation in nursing, elementary school reaching, librarianship, and social work through interviews
    • Finds that men do not face discrimination in these occupations, unlike with women in mostly male occupations, but encounter prejudice from individuals outside their professions
    • The data lend strong support for the belief that women’s occupational inequality is more a consequence of sexist beliefs and practices embedded in the labor force than the effect of numerical underrepresentation per se.

    Gender Paradox

    As gender equality increases, so do gender gaps in science. However, this is hypothesized to be due to the existence of gender values which are only intensified when in an environment when they can be more freely expressed, such as in a more wealthy country.

    The Gender-Equality Paradox in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics Education

    • Used an international database on adolescent achievement in science, mathematics, and reading, with a sample size of 472,242
    • Finds that girls performed similarly to or better than boys in science in ⅔ countries, and in nearly all countries, more girls appeared capable of college-level STEM study than had enrolled.
    • The sex differences in the magnitude of relative academic strengths and pursuit of STEM degrees rose with increases in national gender equality.
    • Life-quality pressures in less gender-equal countries prompt girls’ and women’s engagement with STEM subjects

    Gender stereotypes can explain the gender-equality paradox

    • Some scholars have hypothesized that the gender paradox is due to the existence of deeply rooted or intrinsic gender differences in preferences that materialize more easily in countries where economic constraints are more limited.
    • Finds that the stereotype associated math to men is stronger in more egalitarian and developed countries, and is strongly associated with various measures of female underrepresentation in math-intensive fields and can therefore entirely explain the gender-equality paradox.
    • Economic development reshapes rather than suppresses gender norms, with an emergence of new or more forms of social differentiation across genders.

    Gender Gap in STEM

    It appears that the gender gap in STEM is due to a combination of differences in interests and skills, as well as stereotypes. For example, the gender gap in math is small, but the gap in reading is larger, so women might sort themselves more into humanities-oriented fields. However, stereotype threat can interfere with performance in STEM for women.

    Girls’ Comparative Advantage in Reading Can Largely Account for the Gender Gap in Math-Intensive Fields

    • Looks at 300,000 15-year-old students in 64 countries
    • Finds that gender differences in math performance are small, but that the difference between a student performing in reading and math is higher for girls than boys.
    • When this difference is controlled for, the gender gap in students’ intentions to pursue math-intensive studies and careers is reduced by 75%, while gender gaps in self-concept in math, declared interest for math or attitudes toward math entirely disappear.
    • This suggests that gender differences in reading explain the gender differences in pursuit of math, where women go into more reading-intensive fields for a combination of skill and stereotyping reasons.

    Converging evidence that stereotype threat reduces working memory capacity

    • Finds that stereotype threat interferes with test performance for women because it reduces individuals’ working memory capacity

    Joan McKay versus John McKay: Do gender stereotypes bias evaluations?

    • Meta-analysis
    • Finds that the average difference between ratings of male and female authors is negligible
    • This remains so even when other factors such as sex of subject or year of publication are taken into consideration.

    Gender Gap in Education

    There are mixed findings on the gender gap in education, although it appears that biases persist. Most studies shown here find that women are favored in education which partially closes the gender gap in science, although there is one study which suggests otherwise. Corporal punishment is prevalent worldwide even where bans are in place, and boys are more at risk of corporal punishment compared to girls.

    Boys lag behind: How teachers’ gender biases affect student achievement

    • Finds that middle school teachers favor girls in their evaluations, and this has long-term implications on later grades and entry into science.
    • Without teachers’ bias in favor of girls, the gender gap in choosing a science track would be 12.5% larger in favor of boys.

    The Effect of Grading Practices on Gender Differences in Academic Performance

    • Finds that girls are exposed to easier grading than boys
    • Both boys and girls are negatively affected when the teacher practices easy grading
    • The boys’ responses to easy grading are more uniformly negative than the girls’ responses

    Do gender stereotypes reduce girls' or boys' human capital outcomes? Evidence from a natural experiment

    • Finds that male students face discrimination in each subject, which widens the female-male achievement difference that exists in all subjects except for english, and at all levels of curriculum.
    • The size of the difference is very sensitive to teachers’ characteristics, suggesting the bias against male students is the result of teachers’ and not students’ behavior.

    The Gender Gap and Classroom Interactions: Reality and rhetoric?

    • Most teachers believe they give equal treatment to girls and boys, particularly in support of their learning, but interviews with students and classroom observation suggests this is rarely achieved.
    • In most schools, boys appear to dominate certain classroom interactions, while girls participate more in teacher-student interactions which support learning.

    A Systematic Review of Corporal Punishment in Schools: Global Prevalence and Correlates

    • Systematic review of 53 papers
    • Finds that school corporal punishment is prevalent worldwide including where bans are in place, and does not appear to be decreasing over time.
    • It is associated with physical, academic, mental health, and behavioral problems for children
    • Boys, black students (in the US), and students exposed to violence at home were most at risk of corporal punishment
    • Schools with high rates of other disciplinary practices were more likely to use corporal punishment, while those who employed a mental health professional and trained staff in safety procedures were less likely to use corporal punishment.
    • Teacher attitudes favoring corporal punishment, and their use of violence in other contexts, increased risk
    • Low SES of the student or school environment increased risk, while high levels of state social capital reduced risk.

    On The Origins of Gender Human Capital Gaps: Short and Long Term Consequences of Teachers’ Stereotypical Biases

    • Finds that the stereotypical biases that teachers exhibit in favor of boys has effects on their academic achievement relative to girls, which likely affects women’s underrepresentation in advanced courses in math and science required for going into engineering, computer science, and so on.

    Housing

    Men are disproportionately discriminated against in terms of housing.

    Ethnic and Gender Discrimination in Rental Housing Market: Evidence from Meta-Analysis of Correspondence Tests, 2006-2017

    • Meta-analysis and literature review of 25 studies in OECD countries from 2006-2017
    • Finds that discrimination in the rental housing market is against men and minorities.
    • This discrimination mainly comes from agents’ preferences rather than statistical discrimination
    • Real estate agents discriminate significantly less minority applicants than private landlords do.
    • This is discrimination in the form of being chosen by real estate agents or private landlords

    Sentencing

    Sentencing judgements appear to be in favor of women over men.

    The Effects of Physical Attractiveness, Race, Socioeconomic Status, and Gender of Defendants and Victims on Judgments of Mock Jurors: A Meta-Analysis

    • Meta-analysis of experimental research on mock juror judgements
    • Finds that in general, it was better for defendants to be physically attractive, female, and of high SES, although these advantages were nil for some crimes.
    • There were no overall effects of race on judgements, but the effect of race on punishment was strongly moderated by type of crime
    • Effects of victim characteristics on judgements were generally inconsequential, although defendants were at a disadvantage when the victim was female.

    Arrest and Policing Bias

    All else equal, men are more likely to be arrested and are more aggressively policed on average than women.

    The Effects of Suspect Characteristics on Arrest: A Meta-Analysis

    • Meta-analysis on the effect of suspect characteristics on arrest
    • Finds that black individuals, males, and hispanic individuals were significantly more likely to be arrested than white individuals, females, and non-Hispanic individuals
    • These effects persisted across the majority of moderator categories.

    Examining the influence of drivers' characteristics during traffic stops with police: Results from a national survey

    • Finds that young black and hispanic males are at increased risk for citations, searches, arrests, and uses of force after other extralegal and legal characteristics are controlled.
    • However, they weren’t any more likely to have been carrying contraband

    The Impact of Implicit Stereotyping on Offender Profiling: Unexpected Results From an Observational Study of Shoplifting

    • Finds that looking at shoplifting, when trained observers were allowed to deviate from a clearly specified random selection protocol, oversampled shoppers on the basis of race, gender, and perceived age, beyond the degree to which profiling could be rationally justified to be engaged with.

    Gender and Racial/Ethnic Differences in Criminal Justice Decision Making in Intimate Partner Violence Cases

    • Finds that men are disproportionately arrested and prosecuted compared to women in a variety of areas, and in ways that are not accounted for by differential circumstances.

    Rape Accusations

    The legal definition of rape is often not well understood by rape victims, which means that many rapists, end up getting away with their crimes. Most undetected rapists will rape repeatedly, meaning that ensuring women understand what rape is is essential to reducing the prevalence of rape. Moreover, while the incidence of false rape accusations is difficult to measure, it appears to be exceedingly rare. False rape accusations are primarily motivated by emotional gain and to cover up other behavior.

    Assessing Police Classifications of Sexual Assault Reports: A Meta-Analysis of False Reporting Rates

    • Meta-analysis of 7 studies
    • Finds that the rate of false reports of sexual assault is 0.052.
    • However, the total false reporting rate, including both confirmed and equivocal cases would be greater than this 5% rate

    Motives for Filing a False Allegation of Rape

    • Finds that most false rape accusations were primarily motivated by emotional gain
    • Most were used to cover up other behavior such as adultery or skipping school
    • However, some reported more than 1 motive
    • 20% of respondents said they didn’t know why they filed the false report.

    Repeat Rape and Multiple Offending Among Undetected Rapists

    • Finds that the majority of undetected rapists are repeat rapists, and also committed other acts of interpersonal violence.
    • The repeat rapists averaged 5.8 rapes each
    • The 120 rapists studied were responsible for 1225 separate acts of interpersonal violence

    “Miscommunication” and Undergraduate Women’s Conceptualizations of Sexual Assault: A Qualitative Analysis

    • Approximately 60% of legally defined rape victims do not label their experiences as “rape”, and most of them label the experience as “a serious miscommunication”.
    • Finds that over 3/4ths of miscommunication-labeled victims reported that one or more of the following factors influences their labelling: victim and perpetrator substance use, sexual activity prior to the rape, and perceptions that one did not express nonconsent strongly enough and that the perpetrator did not realize their lack of desire.
    • While miscommunication-labeled and unlabeled victims reported similar assault characteristics, the extent to which those assault characteristics affected their labeling differed.
    • Those who labeled their experiences as miscommunication gave reasons for their label that centered on factors which reflect inconsistencies between their experiences and stereotypical rape.
    • Misperceptions of rape can be addressed through prevention programming and clinical work.

    Child Custody

    Very weak evidence shown here suggests that child custody judgements are discriminatory against men.

    Maternal Preference in Child Custody Decisions

    • Surveys 149 state judges
    • Finds that the judges consistently exhibited continuing indications of maternal preference

    Domestic Violence

    Women are more likely than men to engage in physical aggression to their heterosexual partner, but men are more likely than women to inflict an injury while doing so. There are high similarities in men and women who abuse in relationships. Video dramatization interventions have been shown to reduce violence against women.

    Sex differences in aggression between heterosexual partners: a meta-analytic review

    • Meta-analysis of sex differences in physical aggression to heterosexual partners
    • Women were slightly more likely than men to use one or more act of physical aggression and to use such act more frequently
    • Men were more likely to inflict an injury, and overall 62% of those injured by a partner were women

    Women Who Perpetrate Intimate Partner Violence: A Review of the Literature With Recommendations for Treatment

    • Finds greater similarities than differences for men and women who use abuse in relationships, and women share many of the same characteristics as male offenders, including similar motives and psycho-social characteristics.

    Countering violence against women at scale: A mass media experiment in rural Uganda

    • Looks at a video dramatization intervention in Ugandan villages to reduce violence against women
    • Finds that several months after the investigation there was no change in attitudes condoning violence against women but a substantial increase in willingness to report to authorities, especially among women, and a decline in the share of women who experienced violence.

    Political Representation

    Women perform just as well when they run for public office, but there is a gender gap in political ambition. This is due to a combination of women being less likely to be encouraged or recruited for office, and that public office careers have traits which women tend to find undesirable.

    Why Are Women Still Not Running for Public Office?

    The Continued Underrepresentation of Women in Politics

    • When women run for public office, they perform just as well as man, but they remain severely under-represented still
    • This is because they don’t run for office
    • There is a substantial gender gap in political ambition.
    • Finds that this holds regardless of age, partisan affiliation, income and profession
    • This is due to several factors:
      • Women are less likely to than men to be willing to endure the rigors of a political campaign
      • They are less likely to be recruited to run for office
      • They are less likely to have the freedom to reconcile work and family obligations with a political career
      • They are less likely to think they are qualified
      • They are less likely to perceive a fair political environment
      • Women are less likely to receive the suggestion to run for public office

    Genital Mutilation

    Female genital mutilation has remained stable in many countries over time. While many argue that male genital mutilation is unjustifiable ignored while female genital mutilation stays in the media spotlight, there are important health differences between the two which make them difficult to compare.

    Female genital alteration: a compromise solution

    • Points out that despite 30 years of advocacy, non-therapeutic female genital alteration in minors is stable in many countries
    • Suggests that advocacy should shift away from banning it as a general practice and more towards banning the more dangerous forms of it, as to be culturally sensitive and less discriminative of gender.

    Female Genital Cutting: Considerations for the Western Physician (Warning: NSFL image at the bottom of genital mutilation)

    Circumcision in boys and girls: why the double standard?

    • Argues that there exist key differences between female genital cutting and male genital cutting.
    • There is some evidence that male circumcision may provide medical benefit
    • There is little evidence of significant harm on physical or psychological health, and male circumcision leads to decreased risk of HIV acquisition, other STIs, and urinary tract infections.
    • Moreover, there are far more extreme types of female genital cutting than male circumcision and with them the possibility of far more complications.

    Male Disposability

    Men have worse sentences for the same crime, are treated with less empathy when they experience victimization, and are less likely to be adopted than women.

    The Determinants of Punishment: Deterrence, Incapacitation and Vengeance

    • In vehicular homicides, victims are basically random.
    • Among these, drivers who kill women get 56% longer sentences, and drivers who kill blacks get 53% shorter sentences.

    Does Victim Gender Increase Sentence Severity? Further Explorations of Gender Dynamics and Sentencing Outcomes

    • Finds that victim and offender gender play a role in sentencing outcomes.
    • Female offenders get shorter sentences and offenders who had male victims get shorter sentences.
    • However, probability of incarceration was the same

    Exploring the Role of Victim Sex, Victim Conduct, and Victim–Defendant Relationship in Capital Punishment Sentencing

    • Looks at how victim sex affects capital punishment sentencing
    • Finds that cases with a female victim who was not involved in illegal activity at the time of the murder and acquaintance female victim cases are most likely to result in a death recommendation.

    Missing White Woman Syndrome: An Empirical Analysis of Race and Gender Disparities in Online News Coverage of Missing Persons

    • Looks at online news coverage of missing persons
    • Finds race and gender disparities consistent with Missing White Woman Syndrome
    • Disparities exist in whether a missing person receives any media attention at all, and in coverage intensity among the missing persons that do appear in the news.

    When a man hits a woman: moral evaluations and reporting violence to the police

    • Finds that there exists a greater moral condemnation of men’s assaults on women and the belief that the victims of these assaults are in greater danger.
    • This does not depend on gender, age, level of education, or political ideology of the respondent

    Gender-Role Stereotypes and Perceptions of Heterosexual, Gay and Lesbian Domestic Violence

    • Finds that domestic violence perpetrated is viewed as more serious if the case has a man than if the case has a woman in it, using the same case and replacing the gender of the offender

    Gender Differences in Empathic Sadness towards Persons of the Same- versus Other-sex during Adolescence

    • Finds that empathic sadness was higher in women
    • Both men and women displayed higher empathic sadness toward women than toward men

    Gender and Racial Biases: Evidence from Child Adoption

    • Finds a gender bias in favor of adoption of female and non-African American babies.
    • Finds that excluding same-sex and foreign couples from the adoption process would reduce the number of adopted babies by 6% and 33% respectively
