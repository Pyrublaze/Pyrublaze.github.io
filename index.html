Racism

General/Misc.

The Color of Supremacy: Beyond the discourse of ‘white privilege’

  • Includes a massive list of ways in which black people are disadvantaged historically and today.

Encyclopedia of Political Economy: Racism

  • Unlike sex, race is a social category with little biological significance, often categorized on the basis of physical appearance with some exceptions like Jews. Although such classifications vary, they consistently establish patterns of domination and subordination.
  • The forms of racial oppression vary among societies, although are often characterized by the overt and dominant role of the state. The trend in the modern period is toward an open system where racial oppression becomes the ordinary workings of a capitalist economy.
  • Racism has existed long before capitalism, and capitalism has upheld it as well as provided the impetus for its destruction in some sense, with the ethos of individualism.
  • Gunnar Myrdal believed that the racism in capitalism reproduces itself in a cycle, where the exploitation of blacks lowered their standard of living and so reinforced the prejudice of whites which rekindled exploitation. However, he believed American individualism would eventually diminish the hold of racism, and also thought that appropriate government policies could improve QOL for blacks and thus reduce prejudice against them.
  • Marxists believe racism is a fundamentally economic problem and not just a psychological problem. However, the initial efforts of Marxists to argue that blacks were a super exploited sector of the working class proved unsuccessful as the relationship between wages and productivity could not be shown to be different for black and white workers, nor did efforts to describe the ghetto as an internal colony bear fruit.
  • Employers must avoid offending the sensibilities of their workers, suppliers, or customers to function effectively in the social environment, and therefore tend to establish a division of labor that mimics existing social hierarchies. The same is true with respect to the structure of the labor market as a whole, such as with the theory of segmented and dual labor markets.
  • Employers and white employees may also face incentives to behave in racist ways. Employers may be able to reduce black bargaining power through racism, in which case, whites would also be hurt through having their bargaining power reduced as well. However, the empirical evidence for this is weak, and whites may also benefit through less competition.
  • Other political economists have analyzed racism as a force which divides the labor market into racial/ethnic enclaves, as groups attempt to insulate themselves from job competition. The distributional effects vary with conditions. Nonetheless, a connection is often drawn between beneficiaries and blame for discrimination which can lay the basis for political judgement, particularly for white workers in the battle against racism.
  • Political economists tend to treat racism as a dynamic phenomenon that quantitatively and qualitatively varies depending on context. They also lay the seeds for optimism, suggesting that policies for full employment, as well as progressive changes in social attitudes, law, and public policy, can reduce racism.
  • The political economy analysis of racism has several limitations. First, it centers on the role of black workers rather than unemployed black people. However, political economists attribute problems of the underclass to lack of opportunities. Second, it tends to ignore forces such as trends in marital and birth patterns, child rearing arrangements, labor market restructuring and inner-city turmoil which have had more impact on black well-being than racism per se. Third, the patterns of inequality have become more complex, and blacks are no longer uniformly affected by racism. Finally, the analysis only looks at blacks rather than other groups.

Bias

Stereotypes/Self-Image

Does appearance matter?: the effect of skin tones on trustworthy and innocent appearances

  • Finds that undergraduate, entry-level criminal justice students judged perceived trustworthiness of darker-skinned people as less than of lighter-skinned people.

For Black men, being tall increases threat stereotyping and police stops

  • Finds that for black men, height increased the likelihood of receiving unjustified attention from the police, although white men may benefit from height

Black Neighbors, Higher Crime? The Role of Racial Stereotypes in Evaluations of Neighborhood Crime

  • Finds that the perception of crime in a neighborhood is positively associated with the percentage of young black people living there, even after controlling for crime and other neighborhood characteristics

Whites See Racism as a Zero-Sum Game That They Are Now Losing

  • White people are increasingly seeing racism as a zero-sum game, such that white people view decreases in perceived bias against blacks over the past 6 decades are associated with increases in perceived bias against whites, a relationship not observed in blacks’ perceptions.
  • These changes in whites’ conceptions of racism are extreme enough that whites have now come to view anti-white bias as a bigger societal problem than anti-black bias

Investigating the cognitive structure of stereotypes: Generic beliefs about groups predict social judgments better than statistical beliefs

  • Looks at generic vs statistical stereotypes
  • Finds that generic stereotypes are generally more influential on people’s expectations and explanations for unfamiliar individuals’ traits, and more so when the person displays an authoritarian personality and when they think intuitively rather than analytically

Effectiveness of Stereotype Threat Interventions: A Meta-Analytic Review

  • Meta-analysis of interventions to address stereotype threat
  • Finds that belief-based and identity-based stereotype threat interventions (changing the stereotypical beliefs about the group or group identification) are more effective in reducing stereotype threat

People See Black Men as Larger, More Threatening Than Same-Sized White Men

  • Finds that people see same-size black men as larger and more threatening than same-size white men.
  • Even black men were subject to this effect, although they didn’t see the black men as more threatening than the white men

Race and self-esteem: meta-analyses comparing whites, blacks, Hispanics, Asians, and American Indians and comment on Gray-Little and Hafdahl (2000)

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that blacks scored higher than whites in terms of self-esteem, but whites scored higher than Asians, HIspanics, and American Indians.
  • Most of these differences were small in childhood but grew with age
  • Blacks’ self-esteem was originally smaller than whites until the 1980s.
  • Relative to whites, minority males had lower self-esteem than minority females, and black and Hispanic self-esteem was higher in groups with high socioeconomic status
  • These results are most consistent with a cultural explanation for racial differences in self-esteem

The Structure of Ethnic Identity of Young Adolescents from Diverse Ethnocultural Groups

  • Finds that ethnic identity was associated positively with psychological well-being across racial groups

Improving intergroup attitudes and reducing stereotype threat: An integrated contact model

  • Argues that intergroup contact can be an effective method by which stereotype threat can be reduced.

The Essence of Innocence: Consequences of Dehumanizing Black Children

  • Finds that black children are seen as older and less innocent and prompt a less essential conception of childhood than white same-age peers.

Implicit Association Test

The implicit association test is not a good metric of any substantial level of racism, as the relationship between results on the test and explicit bias is small. Nonetheless, it has some predictive power.

A Systematic Review of the Impact of Physician Implicit Racial Bias on Clinical Decision Making

  • Systematic literature review
  • Finds that the highest-quality studies indicate that while physicians do exhibit an implicit racial bias for whites on the IAT, this does not appear to impact their clinical decision making.

A Meta-Analysis on the Correlation Between the Implicit Association Test and Explicit Self-Report Measures

  • Meta-analysis
  • Suggests that the relationship between implicit bias on the IAT and explicit bias is generally small

Predicting ethnic and racial discrimination: A meta-analysis of IAT criterion studies

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that the IAT is a poor predictor of every explicit measure examined except for brain activity.

Subconscious Bias

This section goes over a lot of somewhat random studies, but the main finding is that subconscious bias appears to exist against black people.

The development of intergroup bias in childhood: How social norms can shape children's racial behaviours

  • Finds that younger and older children will discriminate against black people, but older children will only do so under low salience of anti-racist norms, while younger children will do it in general.

Majority Of White Americans Say They Believe Whites Face Discrimination

  • Finds that 55% of whites believe anti-white discrimination happens in America, while 84% of whites think anti-minority discrimination happens in America.

Keep America Christian (and White): Christian Nationalism, Fear of Ethnoracial Outsiders, and Intention to Vote for Donald Trump in the 2020 Presidential Election

  • Finds that the strongest predictors of supporting Trump in order of magnitude were political party, xenophobia, identifying as African American (negative), political ideology, Christian nationalism, and Islamophobia

Prejudice Is Hard-wired Into The Human Brain, Says ASU Study

  • Finds that prejudice in the form of perceived physical and health danger is hard-wired into the human brain, through it being an adaptive response to perceived threat from outside groups.

Is Multiculturalism or Color Blindness Better for Minorities?

  • Finds that color-blind attitudes among dominant group members, compared to multicultural attitudes, reinforces majority group dominance and minority marginalization

Why Do Interracial Interactions Impair Executive Function? A Resource Depletion Account

  • Finds that interracial interactions impair executive functioning, likely due to resource depletion

https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20658843/

  • Finds that there exists social and economic penalties for counterstereotypic behavior, and this is dealt with by victims through defensive mechanisms which uphold stereotypes about their group identity.

Reactions to ethnic deviance: The role of backlash in racial stereotype maintenance

  • Finds that color-blind attitudes create a preference for individuals which permeates the boundaries of their group identity, while multiculturalism attitudes do not.

Attitudes Toward the Black Political Movement: Trends, Meaning, and Effects on Racial Policy Preferences

  • Finds that whites are less likely than black to support black political movements, and for many whites this is related to other conflict attitudes such as perceptions of conflicting group objectives and fraternal deprivation, all of which are strongly associated with prejudice.

https://www.jstor.org/stable/1519837?seq=1#metadata_info_tab_contents

  • Finds that the perception of black economic deprivation influences the prejudice of whites against blacks, and this exists even when whites understand the structural causes of such economic deprivation

Economic Disadvantage, Status Generalization, and Negative Racial Stereotyping by White Americans

  • Finds that stigmatized students who were high in stigmatized consciousness had lower GPA performance than stigmatized students who were low in stigmatized consciousness, who did not differ from non-stigmatized students

Racial Microaggressions and the Asian American Experience

  • Finds that Asians typically experience different types of microaggressions than other marginalized groups

Racial Microaggressions in Everyday Life: Implications for Clinical Practice

  • Microaggressions appear in the form of microassaults, microinsults, and microinvalidations
  • Almost all interracial encounrters are prone to microaggressions

Criminal Justice

Police Subconscious Bias

The police appear to have a subconscious bias against black people in terms of attributions about the causes of crime and general respect. These don’t appear to arise from overt bias but unexamined beliefs, which can be alleviated through better training and better collection and leveraging of data.

Language from police body camera footage shows racial disparities in officer respect

  • Uses footage from body-worn cameras and computational linguistic algorithms
  • Finds that officers speak with consistently less respect toward black vs white community members, even after controlling for the race of the officer, the severity of the infraction, the location of the stop, and the outcome of the stop.

Strategies for Change: Research Initiatives and Recommendations to Improve Police-Community Relations in Oakland, Calif.

  • Finds that OPD officers treat people of different races differently although these mostly arose from unexamined cultural norms, beliefs, and practices, as opposed to overt bias
  • Suggests that regular review of policies, practices, and procedures, better training of new officers, and better collection and leveraging data, can go a long way to reducing these disparities

Racial Disparities in Official Assessments of Juvenile Offenders: Attributional Stereotypes as Mediating Mechanisms

  • Finds that officers have differences in their attributions about the causes of crime by white vs minority youths, which contributes significantly to the differential sentencing outcomes that exist through sentencing recommendations

Use of Force

This section doesn’t distinguish between the validity of certain studies, and studies looking at use of force vary heavily in terms of their results. However, there is nonetheless a slant towards the idea that minorities do unjustifiably experience more use of force incidents, at the very least because the likelihood of encounters with the police is more common.

A Social Scientific Approach toward Understanding Racial Disparities in Police Shooting: Data from the Department of Justice (1980-2000)

  • Study of 213 metropolitan areas over a 21-year period
  • Finds that even after controlling for racial differences in criminal activity (a proxy for behavior), officers are still more likely to shoot black suspects

Risk of being killed by police use of force in the United States by age, race–ethnicity, and sex

  • Among black men, police use of force is one of the leading causes of death

Police-Related Deaths and Neighborhood Economic and Racial/Ethnic Polarization, United States, 2015–2016

  • Finds that police-related death rates are highest among neighborhoods which are lower in income and which have a higher proportion of residents of color there.

Police killings and their spillover effects on the mental health of black Americans: a population-based, quasi-experimental study

  • Finds that increased exposure to more police killings of unarmed black Americans recently decreases the mental health of Black americans.

Do White Law Enforcement Officers Target Minority Suspects?

  • Finds that although minority suspects are disproportionately killed by police, white officers appear to be no more likely to use lethal force against minorities than non-white officers.

An Empirical Analysis of Racial Differences in Police Use of Force

  • Finds that blacks and hispanics are disproportionately likely to experience both lethal and non-lethal use of force
  • Controlling for variables which take into account context and civilian behavior does not fully explain the disparity in non-lethal use of force, but does fully explain the lethal use of force disparity.

Race and Reasonableness in Police Killings

  • Finds that differences in circumstances do not explain the disparity between black and whites in police killings.
  • The standards in constitutional case law fail to anticipate the circumstances surrounding these types of interactions
  • Enhanced police training appears to be ineffective, likely because it does not take into account the importance of race in officers’ perceptions of risk and their decision-making.

A Multi-Level Bayesian Analysis of Racial Bias in Police Shootings at the County-Level in the United States, 2011–2014

  • Finds that racial bias is more likely to emerge in metropolitan areas with more black people and lower incomes, especially when there is high financial inequality in the county.
  • There was no relationship between bias and crime rates, suggesting that differential crime rates do not explain this bias.

Officer characteristics and racial disparities in fatal officer-involved shootings

  • Finds no racial disparity in shootings at all by officers
  • Officer race does not appear to affect this disparity
  • However, a retraction to the study states that this study does not say anything about whether there is a racial disparity in shootings at all by officers, only whether officer race has any effect.

Is There Evidence of Racial Disparity in Police Use of Deadly Force? Analyses of Officer-Involved Fatal Shootings in 2015–2016

  • Finds that looking at the racial disparity in shootings as a proportion of crime rather than population suggests that there is no racial disparity in fatal shootings by police.

Racial Disparities in Police Use of Deadly Force Against Unarmed Individuals Persist After Appropriately Benchmarking Shooting Data on Violent Crime Rates

  • Responds to the previous study suggesting that benchmarking police killing on relative crime rates rather than relative populations makes police use of force unbiased
  • Finds that controlling for this still creates a large and statistically significant disparity negatively affecting black people.

Does Race Matter for Police Use of Force? Evidence from 911 Calls

  • White officers increase use of force more than black officers when in minority neighborhoods, but not in white or mixed neighborhoods
  • Black and hispanic civilians are five times more likely to experience gun use of force by white officers compared to if white officers scaled up use of force similarly to black officers.

Police Use of Force as an Extension of Arrests: Examining Disparities across Civilian and Officer Race

  • Finds that black civilians are more likely to be involved in a use of force incident during an arrest, but this stems from differences in initial likelihood of arrest.

On Racial Disparities in Recent Fatal Police Shootings

  • Finds that a racial disparity in shooting victims persists even after controlling for local population and arrest demographics.
  • There is no evidence that police-worn body cameras impact the racial distribution of victims.

The Police Officer’s Dilemma: A Decade of Research on Racial Bias in the Decision to Shoot

  • Literature review
  • Finds that while police officers are affected by target race in some respects, they do not generally show a pattern of biased shooting, and this performance depends on the exercise of cognitive control, which allows officers to overcome the influence of stereotypes.

Resolution of apparent paradoxes in the race-specific frequency of use-of-force by police

  • Finds that while there is a racial bias against white individuals with officers' probability to shoot and use lethal force, this is reversed when accounting for the racial disparity in probability of police encounter.

The Reverse Racism Effect: Are Cops More Hesitant to Shoot Black Than White Suspects?

  • Experiment
  • Finds that while there is clear evidence of an implicit bias against black suspects by police, officers were slower to shoot armed black suspects than white suspects and less likely to shoot unarmed black suspects than unarmed white suspects.

Explaining the Disparity in Results Across Studies Assessing Racial Disparity in Police Use of Force: A Research Note

  • Literature review
  • Finds that there is high variation in findings of police use of force studies, even in laboratory settings. The variations could reflect variation in the actual phenomenon across agencies or geographic areas, or could reflect different research methods used to study the same phenomenon.

BLM

BLM protests have been overwhelmingly peaceful, may have had mixed effects on crime, and did not increase COVID in areas where they were.

A Year of Racial Justice Protests: Key Trends in Demonstrations Supporting the BLM Movement

  • The BLM movement has been overwhelmingly non-violent, with 94% of all BLM demonstrations being peaceful

Black Lives Matter's Effect on Police Lethal Use-of-Force

  • Finds that BLM protests have decreased police homicides and property crimes within the areas they are located, but have increased murders in general.
  • However, demographic imbalance in protest variation and treatment timing warrant a cautious interpretation

Black Lives Matter Protests and Risk Avoidance: The Case of Civil Unrest During a Pandemic

  • Finds that BLM protests did not cause rise in COVID within the areas where they were because they resulted in activities being averted which were more risky.

Death Penalty Racism

Race plays a significant role in the probability for a person to get the death penalty, all else equal.

The Role of Race in Washington State Capital Sentencing, 1981-2014

  • Looks at the role of race in washington state capital sentencing
  • Finds that although the race of defendant or victim does not affect prosecutorial decision-making, the death sentence is 4 times more likely to be imposed if the defendant is black

Race and Wrongful Convictions in the United States

  • Finds that despite making up only 13% of the American population, African Americans constitute 47% of the 1900 exonerations listed in the National Registry of Exonerations.

Looking Deathworthy: Perceived Stereotypicality of Black Defendants Predicts Capital-Sentencing Outcomes

  • Finds that after controlling for a wide array of factors, in cases involving a white victim, the more stereotypically a black defendant is perceived to be, the more likely they are to be sentenced to death.

Sentencing

There exists substantial sentencing biases against black people.

Criminalizing Race: Racial Disparities in Plea Bargaining

  • Finds that white defendants are 25% more likely than black defendants to have their initial charge dropped or reduced to a lesser crime.
  • However, white defendants and black defendants with prior convictions are treated the same
  • This suggests that prosecutors may be using race as a proxy for latent criminality and likelihood of recidivism

A Meta-Analysis of Race and Sentencing Research: Explaining the Inconsistencies

  • Meta-analysis of 71 studies
  • Finds that the magnitude of the racial disparity in sentencing between blacks and whites is statistically significant but small and highly variable

Arrests and Traffic Stops

Most studies suggest that there is unjust discrimination against black people in terms of arrests and traffic stops. While this does exist, there is a tradeoff nonetheless between racial equity in traffic stops and prevention of crime, suggesting some profiling can be justifiable, although much of it that happens now appears not to be. Black officers appear to be harsher against black suspects, decreasing black victimization but increasing the arrest rate of black suspects.

The Effects of Suspect Characteristics on Arrest: A Meta-Analysis

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that blacks, males, and Hispanics are more likely to be arrested than whites, females, and non-Hispanics.
  • These persisted among the majority of moderator categories

Understanding Racial and Ethnic Disparities in Arrest: The Role of Individual, Home, School, and Community Characteristics

  • Finds that black youth and young adults are significantly more likely to be arrested than whites in the same category, even after controlling for a range of delinquent behaviors.
  • This disparity disappears once the racial composition of the neighborhood is accounted for

Allocating Police Resources While Limiting Racial Inequality

  • There exists a tradeoff between racial equity in traffic stops and prevention of crime.
  • Therefore, one cannot get a proportional racial distribution of stops without large decreases in efficiency of targeting high crime areas

Reducing Racial Disparities in Crime Victimization

  • Despite what some argue is over-policing, black Americans are more likely to be victimized than white Americans.
  • Finds that post-litigation affirmative action in police departments increased the share of black officers
  • This reduced black victimization
  • This is likely due to increased responsiveness by police to black victimization

A large-scale analysis of racial disparities in police stops across the United States

  • Analyzes nearly 100 million traffic stops conducted across the US
  • Finds that black drivers are less likely to be stopped after sunset, when a veil of darkness clouds one’s face, suggesting bias in stop decisions
  • The bar for searching black and Hispanic drivers was lower than that for searching white drivers, although legalization of recreational marijuana decreases it somewhat

Race and Officer Decision Making: Examining Differences in Arrest Outcomes between Black and White Officers

Drug War

There is a racial disparity in drug arrests, but an important confound exists which makes it difficult to say whether this is racist in nature, namely the disparity in location of purchase. Weak evidence suggests this doesn’t account for the entire disparity, but more evidence in this section would be needed for a more confident conclusion.

Examining Racial Disparities in Drug Arrests

  • Finds that racial disparities exist in drug arrests, and that these cannot be explained by differences in drug offending, non-drug offending, or residing in the kinds of neighborhoods likely to have heavy police emphasis on drug offending.
  • This suggests that there exists racial bias in drug sanctions

Racial differences in marijuana-users' risk of arrest in the United States

  • Finds that while there exists a massive racial disparity in arrests for marijuana possession offenses, there also exists large disparities in buying outdoors, buying from a stranger, and buying away from homes.
  • The paper does do some back-of-the-envelope calculations which suggest that even controlling for this a disparity in arrests persists, but this is not conclusive whatsoever

Race/Ethnicity Differences in the Validity of Self-Reported Drug Use: Results from a Household Survey

  • Finds that African Americans are less trustworthy in their self-reported drug use compared to whites, even after controlling for potential mediators.

Race, Drugs, and Policing: Understanding Disparities in Drug Delivery Arrests

  • Finds that blacks are significantly overrepresented among drug arrestees in Seattle
  • This can be explained by law enforcement’s focus on crack offenders, the priority places on outdoor drug venues, and the geographic concentration of police resources in racially heterogeneous areas.
  • These practices are not determined by race-neutral factors such as crime rates or community complaints.

The War on Marijuana in Black and White

  • Finds that on average a black person is 3.73 times more likely to be arrested for possession of marijuana than a white person.
  • Moreover, this policing has not reduced marijuana usage or availability, and has been very fiscally costly.

Juries and Jury Selection

Juries appear to be highly racially biased against black people, although racial diversity in a jury removes this completely.

Different Shades of Bias: Skin Tone, Implicit Racial Bias, and Judgments of Ambiguous Evidence

  • Finds that showing participants a photo of a dark-skinned perpetrator introduced racial bias into evaluating evidence in a jury.
  • This predicted guilty/not guilty verdicts for the jury
  • These cues were likely implicit and unaware to the participants.

How Much Do We Really Know about Race and Juries? A Review of Social Science Theory and Research 

  • Literature review
  • Concludes that white participants are in fact more likely to be biased against black suspects
  • However, this often only shows up in cases where the situation is not racially charged to begin with
  • Black participants are more likely to be biased against black suspects in general

The Impact of Jury Race in Criminal Trials

  • Finds that all-white jury pools convict black defendants significantly more than white defendants, but this gap is entirely eliminated when the jury pool has at least one black member

Economics

Employment Discrimination

There is extensive empirical evidence suggesting there is employment discrimination against minorities. Specifications in names common among minorities cause variations in the level of discrimination. Ban-the-box policies and employers who advertise diversity hiring can perpetuate this discrimination, despite being well-intentioned. The reasons for this are partially due to statistical discrimination, and also partially due to taste discrimination. While the evidence for employment discrimination is very substantive, there is publication bias in the literature nonetheless.

Quantifying the Effect and Examining its Causes

(Quillian et al., 2017) Meta-analysis of field experiments shows no change in racial discrimination in hiring over time (Abstract and Appendix)

  • Meta-analysis of 28 studies
  • Finds that since 1989, whites on average receive 36% more callbacks from job applications than African Americans, and 24% more callbacks than Latinos
  • There was no change in the 25 years for African Americans, but there was for Latinos.
  • An appendix looked at how covariates, such as criminal record could affect the results, and concluded that “there are no trends in the covariates that appear potentially problematic for our results.”

(Zschirnt & Ruedin, 2016) Ethnic discrimination in hiring decisions: a meta-analysis of correspondence tests 1990–2015 (Full Paper)

  • Meta-analysis of 43 studies
  • Finds substantive evidence of racial discrimination in hiring against minority applicants of all races, and is also robust to the generation of an immigrant.
  • Given the similar hiring decisions for immigrants of different generations, this suggests that there is taste-based discrimination
  • However, statistical discrimination also appears to play a role, as shown by differences in German-speaking countries where more extensive application material is the norm and from public sector employers where application material is more standardized, there is more attention to diversity, and non-discriminatory hiring practices are often explicitly sought.

  • There is publication bias in the literature, as shown by the graph below (black dots are individual studies and white dots are results needed to balance the picture

Effect of Differing Names

(Gaddis, 2017) How Black Are Lakisha and Jamal? Racial Perceptions from Names Used in Correspondence Audit Studies (Abstract)

  • Survey experiment
  • Finds that names given more commonly by highly educated black mothers, such as Jalen and Nia, are less likely to be perceived as black than names given by less educated black mothers, such as DaShawn and Tanisha.
  • This suggests that evidence on racial discrimination operates under a false assumption that all black names are alike.

(Darolia et al., 2015) Race and gender effects on employer interest in job applicants: new evidence from a resume field experiment (Full Paper)

  • Resume experiment
  • Assigns names to the resumes that convey race and gender but for which a strong negative socioeconomic connotation is not implicated:
  • “We selected last names suggestive of race for black, Hispanic and white applicants. Applicants’ first names were used to convey gender, and in the case of Hispanic applicants also were indicative of ethnicity. Specifically, we used three female-sounding first names – Isabella, Megan and Chloe – and three male-sounding first names – Brian, Carlos and Ryan. The first names Isabella and Carlos were paired with Hernandez and Garcia to suggest Hispanic origin, while Chloe and Ryan were paired with Washington and Jefferson to indicate an African American applicant. For white applicants, the first names Megan and Brian were paired with Anderson and Thompson.”
  • Finds little evidence of systematic employer preferences for applicants from particular race or gender groups.

Effect of Diversity Hiring Policies

(Kang et al., 2016) Whitened Résumés: Race and Self-Presentation in the Labor Market (Abstract)

  • Many racial minorities will engage in the practice of whitening their resumes by concealing or downplaying racial cues in their job applications.
  • However, when targeting an employer that presents itself as valuing diversity, minority job applicants engage in relatively little resume whitening, but organizational diversity statements are not actually associated with reduced discrimination against unwhitened resumes
  • Therefore, minorities may be particularly likely to experience disadvantage when applying to ostensibly pro-diversity employers.

Effect of “Ban-the-Box” Policies

(Agan & Starr, 2016) Ban the Box, Criminal Records, and Statistical Discrimination: A Field Experiment (Abstract)

  • Looks at the effect that “Ban-the-Box” policies (policies which restrict employers from asking about applicants’ criminal histories on job applications) have on employment discrimination.
  • Finds that following the implementation of BTB policies in New Jersey and New York City the race gap goes from whites receiving 7% more callbacks than similar black applicants to a 45% difference.

Housing

There is a large amount of evidence that there is discrimination in housing loans and that black neighborhoods are devalued all else equal. Segregation during redlining has had lasting effects, with housing patterns being primarily driven by continuing prejudice and discrimination. This has resulted in black households struggling to make up lost ground relative to white households following the great recession.

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12552-019-09276-x

  • Looks at time series results from 16 field experiments of housing discrimination and 19 observational studies of mortgage lending discrimination
  • Finds evidence of a decline in housing discrimination from the late 1970s to the present
  • The decline is strongest for discrimination involving direct denial of housing availability, and weaker for measures reflecting the number of units recommended and inspected, and significant discrimination remains for these outcomes.
  • Racial gaps in loan denial have declined only slightly, and racial gaps in mortgage cost have not declined at all, suggesting persistent discrimination

https://www.aclu.org/files/field_document/discrimlend_final.pdf

  • Finds that among families that owned homes, white households have started to rebound from the worst effects of the great recession while black households are still struggling to make up lost ground.

https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0003122410380868

  • Finds that segregation was an important contributing cause to the foreclosure argument which was a highly racialized process that disproportionately negatively affected minority clients.

https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/2018.11_Brookings-Metro_Devaluation-Assets-Black-Neighborhoods_final.pdf

  • Finds that homes of similar quality in neighborhoods with similar amenities are worth 23% less in majority black neighborhoods, compared to those with very few or no black residents.
  • Metropolitan areas with greater devaluation of black neighborhoods are more segregated and have less upward mobility for the black children who grow up in those communities.

https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.0735-2166.2004.00205.x

  • Finds that prejudice and housing market discrimination are the primary driver of black-white segregation pattetrns, and that household-level socioeconomic and demographic characteristics explain only a small proportion of segregation

Education

There is heavy racial bias against black students in terms of disciplinary action within schools. The exposure to police violence can affect school performance among black students, and black students report colleges being hostile to them. Programs that can lower the racial achievement gap include comprehensive school reform, charter schools like those from the Harlem Children’s Zone, and school desegregation and racial diversity. Affirmative action exists in some university programs and discriminates against white people. In the US, public education spending per pupil is roughly equal among racial lines. Black and Hispanic parents are more likely than white parents to value a college degree.

School Deferred: When Bias Affects School Leaders

  • Principals endorse more severe discipline for black students compared with white students for the same behavior due to black students being more likely to be labeled as a troublemaker than white students

The Social Structure of Criminalized and Medicalized School Discipline

  • Finds that schools and districts with relatively larger minority and poor populations are more likely to implement criminalized disciplinary policies and less likely to medicalize students through behavioral plans.
  • However, district-level economic disadvantage moderates the influence of school racial composition on criminalized school discipline and medicalization.

The Effects of Police Violence on Inner-City Students

  • Finds that exposure to police violence leads to persistent decreases in GPA, increased emotional disturbance and lower rates of high school completion and college enrollment.
  • These effects are driven entirely by black and Hispanic students in response to police killings of other minorities and are largest for incidents involving unarmed individuals.

“Assume the Position . . . You Fit the Description”: Psychosocial Experiences and Racial Battle Fatigue Among African American Male College Students

  • Documents experiences of 36 black male students at US universities
  • Finds black males were defined as being out of place
  • Students reported psychological stress responses symptomatic of racial battle fatigue
  • There was unanimous agreement that the college environment was more hostile toward black males than other groups.

Comprehensive School Reform: Meta-Analytic Evidence of Black-White Achievement Gap Narrowing

  • Meta-analysis of 34 results from studies
  • Finds that comprehensive school reform (CSR) programs lowered the black-white achievement gap more than with matched traditional schools.
  • These effects substantially to completely eliminated the achievement gap between African American and non-Hispanic white students in elementary middle schools.
  • Title I policies before or after the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001 had no impact on the achievement gap.

Are High-Quality Schools Enough to Increase Achievement among the Poor? Evidence from the Harlem Children's Zone

  • Finds that Harlem Children’s Zone charter schools closed the black-white achievement gap in mathematics

Diversity Associated With Reductions in the Achievement Gap: Data Mining the 2010–2011 New Jersey Assessment of Skills and Knowledge (NJ ASK)

  • Looked at 480 schools districts in New Jersey in 2010-2011
  • Found that the achievement gap between black and white third grade students was lower by over 60% in racially diverse neighborhoods compared to racially homogenous districts.

Long-run Impacts of School Desegregation & School Quality on Adult Attainments

  • Analyzes the life trajectories of children born between 1945 and 1968
  • Finds that for blacks, school desegregation significantly increased both educational and occupational attainments, college quality and adult earnings, reduced the probability of incarceration, and improved adult health status.
  • Desegregation had no effects on whites across each of these outcomes
  • This suggests that the mechanisms through which school desegregation was good for blacks was through improvement in access to school resources reflected in lower class sizes and increases in per-public spending.

The Role of Human Capital in Earnings Differences between Black and White Men

  • Finds that the convergence in educational differences combined with the migration of blacks out of the south since 1940 contributed to the narrowing in the racial gap in earnings over the 1940-80 period.
  • The black-white wage gap has remained considerably larger among men than women.

Racial and Ethnic Preferences at the Three Virginia Public Law Schools

Racial and Ethnic Admission Preferences at Arizona State University College of Law

  • Affirmative action exists in some law schools in the US and discriminates against white people in favor of minorities, often with lower LSAT scores and college GPAs.

Pulling Back the Curtain: Intra-District School Spending Inequality and its Correlates

  • Finds that poor and minority students on average receive 1-2% more resources than non-poor and white students in the same district, but also a large share of districts under-allocate resources to disadvantaged students.
  • Districts that under-allocate resources to poor students relative to non-poor students tend to be poorer and have less income segregation.
  • Districts that under-allocate resources to minority students relative to white students tend to have smaller racial income gaps, less racial segregation, and when it comes to under-allocation to black students, larger white student populations.

The Myth of Racial Disparities in Public School Funding

  • Finds that public education spending per pupil is broadly similar across racial and ethnic groups.
  • To the extent that funding differences exist at all, they tend to slightly favor lower-performing groups, especially blacks.
  • Therefore, unequal student funding cannot be a valid explanation for racial and ethnic differences in school achievement.

Hispanic, black parents see college degree as key for children’s success

  • Black and hispanic parents are more likely to value a college degree than white parents.

Healthcare

There are substantial racial disparities in health, which are partially due to implicit bias against minority groups. These have substantial human and economic costs.

Implicit Racial/Ethnic Bias Among Health Care Professionals and Its Influence on Health Care Outcomes: A Systematic Review

  • Systematic review of 15 studies
  • Most studied use the IAT
  • Finds that low to moderate levels of implicit racial bias were present in all but 1 study
  • Implicit bias was significantly related to patient-provider interactions and health outcomes, but not treatment processes.

Implicit bias in healthcare professionals: a systematic review

  • Systematic review of 42 studies, with a wider methodological range
  • Finds that 35 of them find an implicit bias.
  • All studies that investigated correlations found a significant positive relationship between implicit bias and lower quality of care.

The Economic Benefits of Reducing Racial Disparities in Health: The Case of Minnesota

  • Finds that if racial disparities in preventable deaths were eliminated, the annualized number of lives saved ranges from 475-812 in Minnesota.
  • This translates into $1.2-2.9 billion per year in economic savings.

Estimating the economic burden of racial health inequalities in the United States

  • Finds that eliminating health disparities for minorities would have reduced direct medical care expenditures by about $230 billion and indirect costs associated with illness and premature death by more than $1 trillion for the years 2003-2006.

Racial bias in pain assessment and treatment recommendations, and false beliefs about biological differences between blacks and whites

  • Finds that racial bias exists in pain perception and treatment recommendation accuracy among white laypeople and medical students, which results in racial disparities in pain assessment and treatment recommendations.
  • This reflects false beliefs about biological differences between blacks and whites.

Black Culture

High rates of single parents in the black community are partially due to differences in incarceration and labor market outcomes, which can be solved through policy. Moreover, policy which actively encourages marriage would also be helpful in this process. Rap music, a common alleged factor which negatively affects black culture appears to not contribute to violent crime.

Does Rap Music Contribute to Violent Crime? (READ FULLY)

  • Argues that rap music doesn’t contribute to violent crime and does little more than “tell it like it is”.

Correlates of gang involvement and health-related factors among African American females with a detention history

  • Looks at correlates of gang involvement for African American adolescent girls
  • Finds that low self-esteem, emotional dysregulation, low parental monitoring, poor neighborhood quality, and housing instability, among others, were correlates of gang involvement
  • Finds that gang involvement is independently associated with drug abuse, lower STD prevention knowledge, having a boyfriend in a gang, among other factors

Is Marriage for White People? Incarceration, Unemployment, and the Racial Marriage Divide

  • Argues that the black-white difference in marriages in the US is partially due to the increased risk of black men in terms of incarceration and labor market outcomes.

Mass incarceration and children’s outcomes

  • Finds that black children are 6 times more likely to have or have had an incarcerated parent.
  • This results in black children being more likely to drop out of school, suffer worse educational outcomes, misbehave, and suffer from mental or health problems.
  • One solution mentioned of many is to increase funding for social, educational, and employment programs for released offenders

Marriage: America’s Greatest Weapon Against Child Poverty

  • Argues that marriage is one of the most important tools for helping to eliminate poverty, despite the fact that marriage is becoming less common over time
  • Therefore, policies should be implemented to encourage marriage

The Changing Profile of Unmarried Parents

  • Finds that about 3 in 10 solo parents are black, a large overrepresentation

Model Minority

Positive stereotypes about Asians appear to be harmful to the mental health of Asians, are correlated with negative stereotypes of Asians, and also fail to take into account the diversity among Asians, some who score lower than white people in academics. Family characteristics appear to be the main factor driving the relative academic success of Asians.

The “model minority myth”: Internalized racialism of positive stereotypes as correlates of psychological distress, and attitudes toward help-seeking

  • Finds that higher levels of endorsement of positive Asian stereotypes among Asians was related to higher psychological distress and a worse attitude towards help-seeking.

When Being a Model Minority Is Good . . . and Bad: Realistic Threat Explains Negativity Toward Asian Americans

  • Finds that there is a correlation between positive and negative beliefs about Asians, and that this is due to a perceived threat

Asian Americans as Model Minorities? A Look at Their Academic Performance

Lead Poisoning

There exists racial disparities in lead poisoning which have led to a black disadvantage, at least in the US.

The Racial Ecology of Lead Poisoning: Toxic Inequality in Chicago Neighborhoods, 1995-2013

  • Finds that disparities in lead-poisoning has been a major pathway by which racial segregation has contributed to black disadvantage in the United States
