Immigration

Employment and Wages

 

The overall evidence suggests that immigrants have a small negative aggregate effect on native employment and wages in the short run and a positive effect on both in the long run following labor market adjustment. This short-term negative effect is not distributed evenly across the entire economy but is concentrated in the industries where the immigrants are concentrated in, which is commonly but not always in low-skill industries.

 

Do migrants take the jobs of native workers?

  • Literature review
  • Finds that immigrants do not significantly affect native employment in the short term and boost employment in the long term.
  • Migrants choose locations with available jobs and fill labor shortages
  • They create new jobs by increasing production, engaging in self-employment, easing upward job mobility for native workers, increasing demand, and spurring new businesses to open.

Do immigrant workers depress the wages of native workers?

  • Literature review
  • Finds that most studies for industrialized countries find no effect on wages, on average, and only modest effects on wage differentials between more and less educated immigrant and native workers
  • Native workers’ wages have been insulated by differences in skills, adjustments in local demand and technology, production expansion, and specialization of native workers as immigration rises.
  • There is some evidence of a negative effect of newly settled immigrants on the wages of earlier immigrants
  • Rigid labor markets can reduce the positive effects or cause negative employment effects when combined with immigration.

A Meta-Analytic Assessment of the Effect of Immigration on Wages

  • Meta-analysis of 18 studies
  • Finds no evidence of publication bias in the literature
  • Every 1% increase in the proportion of immigrants in the labor force lowers wages by only 0.119%
  • There is however a large variation between studies and even in estimates within studies

Does Immigration Affect Wages? A Meta-Analysis

  • Meta-analysis of 64 studies
  • Finds a negative effect of immigration on wages that is close to zero.
  • However, this hides large heterogeneity between studies which appears to be mainly a byproduct of differences between countries

Joint Impacts of Immigration on Wages and Employment: Review and Meta-Analysis

  • Literature review and meta-analysis
  • Finds that the local wage and employment effects as a consequence of immigration are very small
  • Employment impact is more pronounced in Europe compared to the US
  • A 1% point increase in immigrants as a share of the local labor market decreases native wages by 0.029% and decreases employment of the native-born by 0.011%.
  • This is at a local level, however, which is smaller than at the national level
  • There are a variety of economic gains through which this small negative effect can be offset however

Job Vacancies and Immigration: Evidence from the Mariel Supply Shock

  • Finds that more immigration reduces the number of job vacancies

Immigration and the Wages of Native Workers: Spatial versus Occupational Approaches

  • Spatial approaches to study the effect of immigration on the wages of native workers tend to find little evidence of a negative effect of immigration on native wages, although this ignores the variation in how immigrants distribute themselves throughout an economy.
  • Once accounting for this through occupational approaches, the effect is negative in those occupations which immigrants move into.

High-Skilled Immigration and the Labor Market: Evidence from the H-1B Visa Program

  • Finds that workers most exposed to increased competition from skilled immigration have lower wages than one would expect given their age and college major
  • A 10 percentage point increase in the immigrant-native ratio of a skill group decreases their relative wages by 1.2%.

THE WAGES OF IMMIGRATION The Effect on the Low-Skilled Labor Market

  • Even after controlling for a variety of factors, immigrants reduce native wages
  • On the aggregate this is a relatively modest effect but when looking at low-skilled occupations as separate the effects are significant
  • A 1% increase in the immigrant composition of an individual’s occupation reduces the weekly wages of natives in the same occupation by about .5%.
  • In low-skilled jobs, a 1% increase reduces native waves by .8%.
  • Because ethnic minorities are more likely to be employed in low-skilled occupation than are native-born whites, they are disproportionately negatively affected
  • In these low-skilled occupations, there is already a racial wage gap between minorities and whites
  • Increases in the size and scope of means-tested entitlement programs and similar measures, while costly, could be beneficial as a response to this
  • Another solution could be limiting the amount of low-skilled immigration
  • Some have also suggested more vigorous enforcement of existing labor laws, although this wouldn’t offset the increase in low-skilled immigration and thus would not help the wage issue substantially. It could still reduce labor exploitation

THE IMPACT OF IMMIGRATION ON THE LABOR MARKET

  • Literature review
  • Finds that most spatial studies suggest that in the long run there is a null or positive effect of immigration on wages, although in the short run before capital adjustment there is a negative effect.
  • Skill-cell studies generally find that an immigration-induced increase in workers within a given skills group lowers the wages and employment of workers with similar skills
  • All of this suggests that immigration can create winners and losers among native workers
