Effects on Crime

 

Higher social spending is associated with significantly lower crime, particularly homicide. This suggests that poverty itself is in fact an important predictor of criminality.

 

The Truly Disadvantaged, Public Assistance, and Crime

  • Reviews and looks at the effects between public assistance and crime. All the studies reviewed say that higher welfare expenditures are associated with less crime, and that more restrictive benefits tended to increase homicide rates. 
  • The authors looked at 406 large metropolitan counties in the United States, adjusting for the cost of living within each county, percent of people over 4 living in different locations over the period of 1985 and 1990, the female labor force participation rate, the unemployment rate, percent of single-person households, percent of the population between the ages of 15 and 29, and an index of urbanism which is the combination of population size and density. 
  • Their models were statistically significant and explained 71% of the variation in total crime, 68% of the variation in property crime, and 63% of the variation in violent crime.

How does the welfare state reduce crime? The effect of program characteristics and decommodification across 18 OECD Countries

  • “The welfare state suppresses crime particularly through social support via generous unemployment benefits. Overall decommodification, the key measure to test IAT, however, does not have any effect on homicide.”

Earned income tax credit and crime

  • “Estimating difference-in-differences models, I find that higher EITCs are associated with significant reductions in violent crime, while not affecting property crimes.”

Spending on social and public health services and its association with homicide in the USA: an ecological study

  • Finds that higher spending on social and public health services is associated with significantly lower homicide rates at the state level

 

Consumption of Drugs/Temptation Goods

 

There is no evidence that at least cash transfers increase the consumption of temptation goods. Existing evidence suggests no net effect.

 

(World Bank, 2014) Cash Transfers and Temptation Goods: A Review of Global Evidence

  • Literature review of 30 studies
  • Finds that almost without exception, cash transfers were found to have no effect on consumption of temptation goods or reduce them.

Life Expectancy/Satisfaction

 

Increased social spending increases life expectancy, general quality of life, and life satisfaction.

 

(Beckfield & Bambra, 2016) Shorter lives in stingier states: Social policy shortcomings help explain the US mortality disadvantage (Full Paper)

  • Looks at the relationship between welfare generosity and life expectancy, using data from 18 high income OECD nations over the period of 1970 - 2010. 
  • After controlling for inflation and PPP adjusted GDP per capita, the percentage of the population aged 65 and older, and the OECD's measure of public and private mandatory social expenditure as a percent of GDP, the coefficient of determination between welfare generosity and life expectancy is 0.167. 
  • The study estimates that if the US had the same welfare generosity as the average of the other 17 OECD nations, the country’s life expectancy would be increased by 3.77 years.

(Fritzell & Nelson, 2014) Welfare states and population health: The role of minimum income benefits for mortality (Full Paper)

  • Looks at the relationship between minimum income benefits and mortality, age standardized death rates, and life expectancy across 18 OECD nations for the period of 1990-2009, finding that countries with higher minimum income benefits tend to do better in all of these outcomes.

(Pacek & Freeman, 2017) The Welfare State and Quality of Life: A Cross-National Analysis (Abstract)

  • Cross-national analysis of advanced democracies
  • Finds that citizens find life more rewarding as the generosity of the welfare state increases, net of economic and cultural conditions

Mental Health

 

While there is a stigma surrounding welfare uptake, evidence suggests that the mental health people who are in need increases when they are given welfare.

 

(Costello et al., 2010) Association of Family Income Supplements in Adolescence With Development of Psychiatric and Substance Use Disorders in Adulthood Among an American Indian Population (Abstract)

  • Finds that a family income supplement in American Indian youth resulted in a lower prevalence of psychopathology compared to the non-exposed, non-Indian population, which persisted into adulthood.

(Griffith, 2004) Progressive Taxation And Happiness (Full Paper)

  • Looks at whether redistributive programs can increase happiness, finding that people with less income gain more from each dollar than people with more income. 
  • This is true whether looking at cross-country longitudinal comparisons or whether looking at within country income distribution. 
  • They also find that a more equal distribution of wealth, as would be created through welfare programs, would maximize the utility of a society, through both theory and cross-country comparisons.

(Krause, 1996) Welfare Participation and Self-Esteem in Later Life (Full Paper)

  • Looks at how welfare programs affect the self-esteem of elderly people, and finds that there is no significant correlation between the two variables until you take into account the interactive statistical effects. 
  • Many elderly people still face self-esteem issues and this problem is disproportionately affecting men.

(Goodban, 1985) The Psychological Impact of Being on Welfare (Full Paper)

  • Looks at how welfare recipients feel about their situation, using a sample of 100 black single mothers on AFDC. 
  • Many of the interviews indicated there was extreme psychological hardship being in the welfare role. 
  • Many of the recipients self-reported that they had faced stigma when receiving welfare and food stamps. 
  • However, because the sample was not randomized, the results may not be generalizable to all welfare recipients.

THE SHORT-TERM IMPACT OF UNCONDITIONAL CASH TRANSFERS TO THE POOR: EXPERIMENTAL EVIDENCE FROM KENYA.

  • Experiment
  • Recipients of unconditional cash transfers experienced large increases in psychological well-being

Nutrition/Growth

 

Welfare, particularly food stamps, improve nutritional quality and improve child physical development

 

(Andreyeva et al., 2018) Dietary Quality of Americans by Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program Participation Status: A Systematic Review (Abstract)

  • Systematic literature review of 25 studies
  • Finds that Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) recipients had equal caloric intake compared to income-eligible non-participants, but that the quality of nutrition increased for recipients.

(World Bank, 2013) Philippines Conditional Cash Transfer Program Impact Evaluation 2012 (Executive Summary)

  • Looks at a Philippines cash transfer program conditional on investment in child care and education as well as use of maternal health services
  • It was found to keep children healthy in terms of nutrition and healthcare

(Hoynes et al., 2016) Long-Run Impacts of Childhood Access to the Safety Net (Abstract)

  • Finds that access to food stamps in childhood leads to a significant reduction in metabolic syndrome and for women an increase in economic self-sufficiency

(Bastagli et al., 2016) Cash transfers: what does the evidence say? (Full Paper)

  • Looks at the effects of cash transfers on health outcomes in developing countries, analyzing 41 studies on a wide range of health outcomes and indicators of positive health outcomes:
  • Cash transfers reduce the occurrence of stunting, but the occurrence of wasting is mixed. The effects on children being underweight are mixed, although the only statistically significant study finds a negative effect.
  • There are no effects on the utilization of health services.
  • Anthropometric outcomes appear to be positive, although there is no statistically significant effect on dietary diversity.

Poverty

 

The overall evidence strongly suggests that higher social spending results in reduced poverty, relative and absolute.

 

(Gould & Wething, 2012) U.S. poverty rates higher, safety net weaker than in peer countries (Full Paper)

  • Looks at social spending and relative poverty rates among 25 selected OECD countries
  • Finds a coefficient of determination of 0.1266 between relative poverty and social expenditure as a percentage of gross domestic product, which indicates that social spending tends to reduce relative poverty rates. The study notes that the relatively low social spending of the United States is one of the reasons why it has a high rate of poverty.

(Kenworthy, 1998) Do Social-Welfare Policies Reduce Poverty? A Cross-National Assessment (Full Paper)

  • Looks at social spending and relative and absolute poverty rates among 15 of the 18 most affluent OECD countries between the period of 1960 and 1991. 
  • Three countries were excluded from the study due to having populations below 3 million. 
  • The absolute poverty rate in this study was 40% of the 1991 US post-tax/transfer median income, while the relative poverty rate was 40% of the 1991 post-tax/transfer median income in each country. 
  • After adjusting for GDP per capita and social welfare policy extensiveness, the coefficient of determination for social spending and absolute poverty rate was .71, while the coefficient of determination for social spending and relative poverty rate was .58, which suggests that social spending reduces both absolute poverty and relative poverty. 
  • These results were statistically significant.

(Smeeding, 2006) Poor People in Rich Nations: The United States in Comparative Perspective (Full Paper)

  • Looks at social spending and relative non-elderly poverty rates across 11 different countries, using a poverty rate defined as half of a nations’ median income, adjusting for household size and purchasing power parity. 
  • The 11 countries were chosen on the basis of having a wide range of high-income countries most comparable to the United States. 
  • The study found a coefficient of determination of 0.6099, suggesting that social spending reduces poverty.

(Burtless & Smeeding, 2007) Poverty, Work, and Policy: The United States in Comparative Perspective (Full Paper)

  • looks at social spending and relative non-elderly poverty rates across 13 industrialized countries, defining poverty as living below 50% of national median income. 
  • They find a coefficient of determination of 0.8245, suggesting that social spending reduces poverty.

(Bastagli et al., 2016) Cash transfers: what does the evidence say? (Full Paper)

  • Looks at the effects of cash transfers on poverty in developing countries, analyzing 44 studies covering 19 countries and 31 cash transfer programs. 
  • The vast majority of the statistically significant results found a reduction in poverty. 
  • This remained true for all metrics of poverty, including total expenditure, food expenditure, poverty headcount, and the squared poverty gap.

Mechanisms of poverty alleviation

  • Finds that across countries, the structure of non-targeted entitlements is more important in explaining differential poverty levels than are means-tested benefits

Crowding Out

 

While a crowding out effect potentially exists between welfare and charitable donations, it is far from a 1:1 crowding out effect, suggesting there is not perfect substitution between social spending and voluntary charitable donations.

 

(Wit & Bekkers, 2016) Government Support and Charitable Donations: A Meta-Analysis of the Crowding-out Hypothesis (Abstract)

  • Meta-analysis
  • Finds that experiments report a -$0.64 of private charity for every dollar spent on welfare, although nonexperimental analyses find +$0.06 of private charity for every dollar spent on welfare
  • Nonetheless, there is no 1:1 crowding-out that exists
